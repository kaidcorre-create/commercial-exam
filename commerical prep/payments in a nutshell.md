### Why Payment Matters in Commercial Law

Payment is the **lifeblood of commercial activity**. Without it, commerce collapses. Key reasons:

- Businesses rely on incoming payment to discharge their own obligations (wages, rent, taxes, supplier payments). Non-payment has a **domino effect**.
- Businesses that provide goods or services first are **exposed to risk** — if unpaid, they may face insolvency or legal action.
- Payment is important for **both sides**:
    - For the **payee/creditor**: to receive what they are owed.
    - For the **payer/debtor**: successfully discharging payment obligations protects them from legal action (enforcement officers seizing assets, etc.).

### Why the _Form_ of Payment Matters

The type of payment method chosen affects:

1. **Discharge of payment obligations** — cash discharges immediately upon hand-over; a cheque only discharges once it is presented and honoured by the bank.
2. **Transferability and circulation** — some documentary payments (bills of exchange, open cheques) can be passed from party to party, supporting commercial liquidity. Bank transfers cannot.
3. **Title and risk allocation** — with cash, title passes immediately; with a cheque, title to the money only passes when the bank pays. The payer remains at risk of legal action until full discharge.

### Key Terminology

#### Documentary Payments

Legally enforceable payment mechanisms (bill of exchange, cheque, promissory note) that substitute for cash. They are recognised by law as valid forms of payment, capable of **fully or partially** discharging a payment obligation.

> _Analogy: Like a gift voucher — the document itself represents a right to receive value._

#### Negotiability vs Transferability

|Feature|Transferable|Negotiable|
|---|---|---|
|Can be passed to next person?|Yes|Yes|
|Next person's title|Same as transferor's (defects pass too)|May be **free of prior defects**|
|Exception to Nemo Dat?|No|**Yes**|

- **Transferability**: passing the instrument on, but the recipient acquires it with any defects it carries.
- **Negotiability**: a _higher_ form of transferability. A bona fide purchaser for value without notice of defects may acquire a **better title** than the transferor — an exception to the _Nemo Dat_ rule (_you cannot give what you don't have_).

> _Analogy: Transferability is tap water; negotiability is filtered tap water — it clears prior defects._

#### Documentary Intangible

A tangible document (e.g., a cheque) that incorporates **intangible property rights** (the right to demand payment or sue). A £50 banknote is a documentary intangible — the paper itself is worthless; its value lies in the right it represents.

#### Chose in Action / Thing in Action

A right enforceable only through legal proceedings (e.g., a debt). Contrast with a **chose in possession** (a tangible thing you can hold and own, like a bike or banknote).

|Type|Example|How enforced|
|---|---|---|
|Chose in possession|Cash, bike|Physical possession/recovery|
|Chose in action|Debt, contractual claim|Legal action (suing)|

#### Right in Rem vs Right in Personam

- **Right in rem**: enforceable against the **whole world** (e.g., ownership of money, goods, negotiable instruments). If someone steals your £100, you can sue anyone who interferes with it.
- **Right in personam**: enforceable only against a **specific person** (e.g., a debt — you can only sue the debtor).

#### Fungibility

Goods/money are **fungible** where an obligation can be satisfied by a substitute that is materially identical in kind and value — you don't need to return the _exact same_ notes lent.

### What Constitutes a Payment?

- Payment obligations arise from: sale of goods, employment contracts, loan agreements, etc.
- Under **Sale of Goods Act s.2(10) and s.28**: payment and delivery obligations are **concurrent** — a party may withhold delivery until the other is ready to pay, and vice versa.
- **Payment ≠ Barter**: Barter (exchanging goods for goods) involves no money component and is not regulated as a payment.

#### Who Can Make Payment?

- Normally the debtor.
- A person with **authority** may pay on the debtor's behalf.
- An **unauthorised person** who pays voluntarily does not automatically acquire a right of reimbursement.

> **Owen v Tate**: A volunteer who paid another's debt without the debtor's knowledge, consent, or subsequent ratification could _not_ recover that sum from the debtor.

#### Acceptance of Payment

- Acceptance may be **express** or **implied** (e.g., if the creditor has access to the funds in their account — _TSB Bank of Scotland_).
- Until accepted, the payment obligation remains outstanding; merely offering payment is insufficient.
- If the contract **specifies a payment method**, the creditor may refuse payment by any other means.
- If no method is specified, the creditor may only reject an offered method if it is **unreasonable** (e.g., attempting to pay £10,000 in coins).

#### Intermediary Risk

Where payment is made through an intermediary (bank transfer, etc.), the debtor's obligation is **not discharged** until the creditor actually receives the funds. If the intermediary defaults, the risk remains with the payer.

> **Charge Card Services**: Traders paid in good faith but payment was invalid because the intermediary became insolvent — the payer's obligation was not discharged.

## Part 2 — Money, E-Money & Bank Transfers (Week 13, Lecture 2)

### Valid Tender vs Legal Tender

|Concept|Definition|
|---|---|
|**Valid Tender**|The payment method the payee contractually agrees to accept (can be anything — card, crypto, etc.)|
|**Legal Tender**|The method the _law_ recognises as sufficient to discharge a payment obligation — for England & Wales: Bank of England banknotes and Royal Mint coins|

- If a debtor offers payment in **legal tender** and the creditor refuses _without_ having specified a different method, the debtor can use that offer as a **defence** against non-payment claims.
- Under the **Coinage Act**, there are limits on using small denomination coins for large debts.

### Other Payment Methods (Brief)

- **Part Exchange**: Payment using a combination of money and goods; the goods must be given an assigned monetary value.
- **Novation**: An existing payment obligation is replaced by a new one. Once novated, the creditor can only pursue the debtor under the _new_ agreement.
- **Set-Off**: Where mutual debts exist, a party can reduce their obligation by the amount owed to them (subject to rules: debts must be liquidated, parties in same capacity, etc.).

---

### Money

Money serves as:

- **Medium of exchange** — standard method of paying for goods/services
- **Unit of account** — common measure of value
- **Store of value** — stable and easy to hold

**Types of Money:**

- **Physical/Fiat money**: coins and banknotes (legal tender in England & Wales)
- **Bank money**: the balance appearing in your bank account
- **Electronic money (e-money)**: digitally stored monetary value (e.g., Apple Pay, Google Pay)

### Characteristics of Physical Money (Banknotes)

- **Legal tender** (subject to contractual override)
- A **physical chattel** — tangible, transferable by delivery
- **Fungible** — the specific notes need not be returned, only an equivalent amount
- **Chose in possession** — enforceable by possession, not legal action
- **Negotiable instrument** — an innocent third party who takes it in good faith, for value, and without notice of any defect acquires good title

> **Miller v Race (1758)**: A stolen banknote was exchanged at an inn by Miller in good faith and for value. The Bank of England tried to refuse him. The court held Miller acquired good title — banknotes are negotiable instruments and an innocent purchaser for value without notice takes free of prior defects.

### Electronic Money (E-Money)

Defined as: _stored monetary value, represented by a claim on the issuer, issued on receipt of funds, for the purpose of making payment transactions, accepted by persons other than the issuer._

Key features:

- You do **not own** the money — it belongs to the issuer (bank/Apple/etc.), but you have a **personal claim** against them to return it on demand.
- **Backed by fiat money** (real currency exists somewhere to support it) — unlike cryptocurrency, whose value is purely demand-driven.
- **Not negotiable** — cannot be freely circulated from person to person like cash.
- Stored digitally (mobile apps, smart cards).

**E-Money vs Cryptocurrency:**

|Feature|E-Money|Cryptocurrency|
|---|---|---|
|Backed by real money|Yes (fiat-backed)|No|
|Value stability|Stable|Volatile|
|Regulatory framework|Regulated|Different/evolving rules|
|Acceptance|Widely accepted|Limited|

### Funds Transfer — Bank Transfers

A cashless transfer from one bank account to another involving **debiting** the payer's account and **crediting** the payee's — no physical movement of cash.

#### BACS (Bankers' Automated Clearing Service)

- Used for **bulk, lower-value** transfers: wages, pensions, regular payments
- Processing time: **3 days** (Day 0: instruction; Day 1: BACS processes; Day 2: payee's bank processes; Day 3: credited)
- **Revocable** up to end of Day 2 (before 3:30 PM), before the payee's bank is instructed
- Owned by banks as a non-profit entity; processes enormous volumes daily (~85 million pounds/day)

#### CHAPS (Clearing House Automated Payment System)

- Used for **high-value, time-sensitive** transfers (house purchases, large commercial settlements)
- Processed in **real time** — same day
- **Not revocable** once sent — money reaches the payee's account almost instantly
- Bank of England acts as the settlement intermediary: banks hold accounts at the Bank of England, and CHAPS instructions trigger debits/credits between those accounts
- Processes ~217,000 transfers/day averaging £400 billion in daily value

> **Fun fact**: BACS and CHAPS together move enough money that in just 9 days, the total equals the UK's entire annual GDP (£3 trillion).

#### When is Payment Discharged?

Payment is discharged when the **payee's bank unconditionally accepts the payment as creditor** — i.e., credits the payee's account. Until that moment, the risk remains with the payer.

#### Payer Mistakes & Bank Liability

> **Philipp v Barclays**: Mr Philipp gave incorrect beneficiary details for a CHAPS payment (~£25–30k). The bank followed his instructions correctly. The court held: where a bank executes instructions _without error_, it has **no liability**. The payer bears the commercial risk of providing incorrect details.

**Responsibilities:**

- **Payers**: must provide correct account details and have sufficient funds.
- **Payees**: entitled to payment once their bank unconditionally credits their account.
- **Banks**: liable only if they execute instructions incorrectly; not for customer error.

> **Title Energy v Bank of England** — also relevant on discharge of payment obligations (read for detail).

## Part 3 — Documentary Payments: Bills of Exchange & Promissory Notes (Week 14, Lectures 1 & 2)

### Documentary Payments — Overview

Documentary payments are a **substitute for cash**, historically developed to enable commerce without physically moving large sums. They remain important in:

- International trade
- High-value domestic transactions (inter-bank settlements, property purchases, large corporate deals)

A documentary payment instrument is always:

- An **unconditional** promise or order to pay
- For a **fixed sum**
- To a **specified payee** (or bearer)

Governed by the **Bills of Exchange Act 1882**.

---

### Two Main Forms of Negotiable Instrument

#### 1. Promise to Pay — Promissory Note (s.83)

The **drawer promises** to pay the payee a fixed sum on a fixed or determinable date.

> _"I promise to pay £50,000 on 15 January 2026."_

Requirements (s.83):

- Fixed sum
- Fixed or determinable date (e.g., "30 days after issue" is valid — calculable)
- Unconditional (no conditions attached — e.g., cannot say "if I sell my stock")
- In writing, signed by the maker

Promissory notes are **negotiable** — they can be passed along the chain until maturity.

> **Claydon v Bradley**: The wording "paid back in full by first of July 1983" (i.e., _by_ a date rather than _on_ a date) introduced uncertainty and **invalidated** the promissory note.

#### 2. Order to Pay — Bill of Exchange / Cheque (s.3 / s.73)

The **drawer instructs a third party (the drawee)** to pay a specified sum to the payee.

> _"I order Chloe Bank to pay Ali £500 on 10 November 2026."_

Three parties: **Drawer → Drawee → Payee**

**Cheque** = a type of bill of exchange where the drawee _must_ be a **bank**. For a general bill of exchange, the drawee can be any person, entity, or even the drawer themselves.

---

### Requirements for a Valid Bill of Exchange (s.3)

|Requirement|Detail|
|---|---|
|Unconditional order|No conditions can be attached to payment|
|In writing|Must be a written document|
|Addressed from one person to another|Drawer to drawee|
|Signed by the drawer|**Most critical element** — no bill exists without it|
|Fixed or determinable time|On demand, on a stated date, or a calculable future date|
|Fixed amount|Must be certain — written in digits and words|
|Payee specified|Named person or "bearer"|

**Acceptance**: Before the drawee is obligated to pay, the **payee** must present the bill to the drawee for acceptance (signing it). Until accepted, the drawee has no obligation. If the drawee refuses acceptance, the payee can immediately pursue the **drawer**.

**On demand**: If no future date is stated, the drawee must pay upon presentation. If they refuse, again, the payee pursues the drawer.

---

### What Does _Not_ Invalidate a Bill (s.3(4))

Commercial law favours keeping instruments valid. The following omissions do **not** invalidate a bill:

- Missing date
- Missing statement of value/consideration
- Missing place of drawing or place of payment

The holder may fill these in, or the Act provides default rules.

---

### Completing an Incomplete or Irregular Bill — Section 20

**Incomplete bill**: Missing material details (amount, date, payee, drawee address — _but not the signature_).

**Irregular bill**: Details are present but the bill has some defect (obtained by fraud, breach of faith, etc.).

**Section 20** allows the **holder** (the person to whom the bill has been transferred) to complete missing details, provided:

- Done within a **reasonable time** (for cheques: 6 months is a common benchmark — after which the bank may treat it as a stale cheque)
- Done **strictly within authority** — express or implied

**Authority**: Section 20 implies authority for the holder to fill in details. But filling in £10,000 when only £1,000 was agreed is beyond authority.

**If completed outside authority or unreasonable time**: the bill is invalid and cannot be enforced — _unless_ acquired by a **holder in due course**.

> **Note**: Signature is the **absolute red line** — a missing or forged signature cannot be cured by Section 20.

---

### How Bills are Transferred — Delivery and Endorsement

|Instrument type|How transferred|
|---|---|
|**Bearer bill** ("pay to bearer")|Delivery alone — no signature required|
|**Order bill** (named payee)|Delivery **plus endorsement** — the holder signs the back and passes it on|

- **Endorsement**: signing the reverse of the instrument, passing it to the next person.
- No notice to the original debtor is required (unlike assignment of debts generally).

---

### Discounting a Bill

A holder can convert a **future payment into immediate cash** by selling the bill to a third party at a discount.

**Example**: Becky holds a £50,000 bill payable on 31 March. In January, needing cash, she sells it to Dana for £47,000. Dana pays Becky £47,000 now and collects £50,000 from the drawee in March — making a £3,000 profit. Becky gets liquidity; Dana gets a return.

This illustrates why documentary payments support commercial **liquidity**.

---

### Holder in Due Course (HDC)

The most protected position. A holder in due course acquires the instrument **free of all prior defects** and is an exception to the _Nemo Dat_ rule.

**Requirements to be an HDC:**

1. Took the bill **before it was overdue**
2. Took it in **good faith** (bona fide)
3. **No notice** of any prior defects, dishonour, or fraud
4. Provided **value** (cannot be a gift)
5. The bill is complete and regular on its face (defects must not be apparent)

An HDC can enforce the bill even if:

- The bill was obtained by fraud, duress, or illegality in an earlier transaction
- The bill was altered (if the alteration is not apparent)
- The bill was incomplete and filled in beyond authority (if not obvious on the face)

> **Exception**: Forged signatures — **no HDC protection**. Ali is never bound by a forged signature. However, subsequent endorsers who endorsed the bill _are_ personally liable (they are stopped/estopped from denying liability).

---

### Limits on Negotiability and Transferability

#### Account Payee Cheques ("A/P")

- Adding "A/P" or "account payee" to a cheque means it **cannot be transferred at all** — the named payee must present it personally to the bank.
- Governed by **s.81A**.

#### Non-Negotiable Cheques

- Writing "non-negotiable" on a cheque means it **can still be transferred** but the next holder cannot acquire a better title than the transferor had — defects follow the instrument.
- Useful for drawers who want to prevent a defective title from being "cleansed" by negotiation.

---

### Material Alteration — Section 64

If the **essential terms** of a bill are altered (amount, date, payee) **without the consent of all liable parties**, the bill is **void** — _unless_:

- All parties assent to the alteration, OR
- The alteration is **not apparent** on the face of the document, in which case an **HDC** can still enforce it at the _original_ terms

---

### Liabilities of the Parties

**Hierarchy of liability** (who the final holder can pursue, in order):

1. **Acceptor / Drawee** — primary liability; once they have accepted the bill, they must pay regardless of any underlying disputes between drawer and payee.
2. **Drawer** — secondary liability; guarantees payment if the drawee fails.
3. **Endorsers** — tertiary liability; each person who endorsed (signed) the bill guarantees payment to subsequent holders.

**Excluding liability — "without recourse"**: An endorser can write "without recourse" when passing the bill on. This excludes them from the chain of liability if the bill is ultimately dishonoured.

**Bearer bills**: Because no endorsement/signature is required, default liability attaches only to the drawer and drawee. Intermediate holders of bearer bills are generally not liable (unless they choose to sign).

**Forged signatures & estoppel**: Even where the original drawer's signature is forged (making the bill void as against them), **subsequent endorsers** who signed are **estopped** from denying liability to the final holder — by endorsing, they personally guaranteed payment.

> **Order vs Bearer on liability**:
> 
> - Order bills: default = must endorse (sign) → automatically in liability chain. Can opt out with "without recourse."
> - Bearer bills: default = no signature needed → not in liability chain. Can opt _in_ by signing.

---

### Notice of Dishonour (s.49)

If the drawee fails to pay, the final holder must promptly send **notice of dishonour** to all endorsers and the drawer. This alerts them to prepare for potential claims.

- Notice can be by phone, email, or formal written means.
- **Failure to give timely notice discharges endorsers** from liability (but not the drawer/acceptor).

**Recourse flows backwards**: The final holder → pursues Dana → Dana can recover from Chloe → Chloe recovers from Becky, etc. Each party can claim compensation from those higher in the original chain.

---

### Key Defences Against Payment Liability

|Defence|Effect|
|---|---|
|**Forged/unauthorised signature**|Bill is void; original drawer has no liability. But endorsers who signed are personally liable (estoppel).|
|**Lack of capacity**|E.g., a minor who drew a bill — not liable to pay.|
|**Material alteration (s.64)**|Bill void if altered without consent of all parties, unless not apparent to an HDC.|
|**Failure of consideration**|The drawer may challenge if the underlying contract consideration was not performed (but this defence does not extend to an HDC).|

---

### Order Bills vs Bearer Bills — Summary

|Feature|Order Bill|Bearer Bill|
|---|---|---|
|Payee designation|Named person|"Bearer" / no name|
|Transfer method|Delivery + endorsement (signature)|Delivery alone|
|Default liability for intermediaries|Yes (signed = liable)|No (unsigned = not liable)|
|Security|Higher (traceable)|Lower (untraceable)|
|Speed of circulation|Slower|Faster|

> **Clutton v Attenborough**: Where a bill was written out in a fictitious person's name, the law treats it as a **bearer bill** — whoever holds it can present it for payment.
