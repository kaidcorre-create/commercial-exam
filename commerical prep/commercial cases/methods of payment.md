


## Owen v Tate [1976] QB 402 #card
- P paid D's bank debt of £350 without D's knowledge or consent. 
- The bank cleared the debt. P then sought reimbursement from D.
- A volunteer who pays another's debt without the debtor's authority, consent, or subsequent ratification has no right to be reimbursed by the debtor.

## TSB Bank of Scotland plc v Welwyn Hatfield DC [1993] 2 Bank LR 267 #card
- Funds were transferred to the payee's bank account and became unconditionally accessible to the payee
- no express acceptance was communicated.
- Acceptance of payment can be implied from conduct

## Miller v Race (1758) 1 Burr 452 #card
- A banknote worth £21 10s was stolen
- It passed to Miller, who took it in good faith, for value, and in the ordinary course of business, without knowing it was stolen. 
- The Bank of England refused to honour it when Miller presented it.
- Banknotes are negotiable instruments. 
- A bona fide purchaser for value without notice of any defect acquires good title even if the note was stolen
- an exception to the Nemo Dat rule.

## Re Charge Card Services Ltd (No 2) [1989] Ch 497 #card
- Traders accepted a charge card as payment for goods. T
- he card issuer (the intermediary) subsequently became insolvent before completing payment to the traders.. 
- The payer's obligation is not discharged until the creditor actually receives the funds
- if the intermediary defaults, the risk stays with the payer.

## Aldridge v Johnson (1857) 7 E & B 885 #card
- The parties agreed to exchange cattle for barley, with a money adjustment for the difference in value
- Where a monetary value is placed on goods offered in part payment of a money obligation, this constitutes payment (not barter). 
- money obligations can be discharged by an equivalent amount, not necessarily the identical notes or coins.

## Stein v Blake [1996] 1 AC 243 #card
- Two companies in an insolvency situation had mutual cross-claims against each other. 
- One party sought to enforce its claim independently without accounting for what it owed to the other.
- In insolvency, mutual debts are automatically set off against each other.

## Torkington v Magee [1902] 2 KB 430 #card
- A contractual right (an option) was purported to be assigned. 
- The question was whether such an intangible right was capable of assignment.
- A chose in action is a right in property that can only be claimed or enforced by legal action, not by taking physical possession.

## Foley v Hill (1848) 2 HLC 28 #card
- A customer deposited money with a bank at interest. 
- The customer later sought to recover the money as a trust fund rather than a simple debt.
- Money paid into a bank account belongs legally to the bank, not the customer
- . The relationship between bank and customer is one of debtor and creditor

## Foskett v McKeown [2001] 1 AC 102 #card
- Trustees misappropriated trust money and used it to fund life insurance premiums. 
- Beneficiaries sought a proprietary claim over the insurance payout.
- Confirms that money paid into a bank account belongs legally and beneficially to the bank, not the account holder (per Lord Millett).

## Tayeb v HSBC [2004] EWHC 1529 (Comm) #card
- A customer instructed HSBC to make a large funds transfer.
- HSBC initially refused, citing concerns about the source of the funds.
- A payment by funds transfer is completed once the payee's bank unconditionally accepts the payee as its creditor in respect of the amount transferred 
- A bank owes a duty to execute payment instructions promptly and correctly; unjustified refusal may attract liability.


---

## Tidal Energy Ltd v Bank of Scotland Plc [2014] EWCA Civ 1107 #card
- Tidal Energy instructed Bank of Scotland to make a funds transfer but provided incorrect account details (wrong sort code and account number). 
- The bank followed the instructions exactly and the money reached the wrong person.
- The payer's bank acts as the payer's agent based on the contract between them and the rules of the payment system used. Where the payer provides incorrect beneficiary details and the bank follows those instructions, the payment obligation is not discharged — the money did not reach the intended creditor. The bank is not liable if it correctly executed the instructions as given; the payer bears the risk of providing incorrect details.

## Philipp v Barclays Bank UK plc [2023] UKSC 25 #card
- Mr Philipp was deceived by fraudsters into authorising a CHAPS transfer of approximately £700,000 to accounts controlled by the fraudsters. 
- Barclays executed the instructions exactly as given. Mr Philipp sued Barclays for the loss.
- A bank that correctly executes a customer's own authorised payment instructions bears no liability for resulting loss

## Banque Cantonale de Geneve v Sanomi [2016] EWHC 3353 (Comm) #card
- A bill of exchange was transferred through a chain of parties.
- Questions arose as to whether a later party could enforce it as a holder in due course given allegations of serious fraud in an earlier transaction.
- Affirms the holder in due course principle but adds a caveat: 
	- where there is evidence of serious or obvious fraud, courts will scrutinise the transaction more carefully and the negotiability protection may not fully apply. 
	- The HDC principle from Miller v Race is not absolute
	- strong fraud can lead courts to look behind the instrument.

## Roberts & Co v Marsh [1915] 1 KB 42 #card
- The drawer of a cheque wrote 'to be retained' on it
- a note addressed to the payee asking them to hold off presenting it to the bank, not a condition directed at the bank.
- A note or instruction on a cheque addressed to the payee (not the drawee/bank) does not invalidate the cheque. 
- The bank's order remains unconditional.

## Clutton v Attenborough & Son [1897] AC 90 #card
- A dishonest clerk fraudulently filled in the name of a fictitious, non-existent person as the payee on several bills of exchange, then negotiated them.
- A bill of exchange made payable to a fictitious or non-existent person is treated as a bearer bill.
- It may be transferred by delivery alone, without endorsement, and whoever holds it may present it for payment.

## Greenwood v Martins Bank Ltd [1933] AC 51 #card
- Greenwood's wife forged his signature on cheques over a period of time. 
- Greenwood discovered the forgeries but said nothing until after his wife's death. 
- The bank had paid out on all the forged cheques. Greenwood then sought to recover the sums from the bank.
- A customer who knows their cheques are being forged but fails to notify the bank promptly is estopped from later asserting the forgery against the bank to claim reimbursement. 
- Silence amounted to a representation to the bank that the cheques were genuine, on which the bank relied to its detriment.

## Claydon v Bradley [1987] (cited in textbook as authority on promissory notes) #card
- A promissory note contained the wording "paid back in full by first of July 1983", using the word "by" rather than "on" a specific date.
- A promissory note must contain a certain or determinable payment date.
- The word "by" a date introduces uncertainty (payment could be made at any point up to that date) 
- it invalidates the promissory note. The date must be specific and certain.


---

## Hong Kong and Shanghai Banking Corp Ltd v GD Trade Co Ltd [1998] CLC 238 #card
- The drawer of a bill of exchange imposed conditions on the payee in addition to the order given to the drawee/bank.
- Conditions imposed on the payee by the drawer do not affect the validity of a bill of exchange, provided the instruction to the drawee remains unconditional. 
- It is only the order to the drawee that must be unconditional
- the drawer may impose separate conditions on the payee without compromising the instrument. (Confirms Roberts & Co v Marsh.)

## St Vincent European General Partner Ltd v Robinson [2018] EWHC 1230 (Comm) #card
- A dispute arose about the appropriate method of tendering payment of a very large commercial debt.
- In modern commercial transactions, parties are not expected to tender payment in cash or coins for large debts. 
- No sensible business would expect a debtor owing millions to arrive with a briefcase of banknotes

## Barclays Bank Ltd v Quistclose Investments Ltd [1970] AC 567 #card
- A company borrowed money from Quistclose specifically to pay a dividend. 
- The money was paid into a separate account at Barclays. 
- Before the dividend was paid, the company went into liquidation. Barclays sought to set off the funds against the company's overdraft.
- Where money is paid to a party (including a bank) for a specific, identified purpose on the understanding it may not be used for any other purpose, a trust arises in favour of the payer if that purpose fails.
- This gives the payer a proprietary claim rather than merely an unsecured debt claim

## Dawson (GJ) (Clapham) Ltd v Dutfield [1936] 2 All ER 232 #card
- A transaction involved the exchange of goods together with a money component
- a part exchange arrangement in a commercial context.
- Part exchange constitutes a form of payment (not barter) where a monetary value is assigned to the goods being exchanged.

## Rolls Razor Ltd v Cox [1967] 1 All ER 397 #card
- A company sought to exercise set-off against a debt owed to a salesman, but the debts arose in different capacities.
- For set-off to operate, there must be mutuality.
- Set-off cannot be used where one of the claims arises in a different capacity or is not a money claim.

## Bills of Exchange Act 1882 — s.3: Requirements for a valid bill of exchange #card 
A bill of exchange must be:
1. An **unconditional order in writing**
2. Addressed by one person to another
3. **Signed by the drawer**
4. Requiring the drawee to pay **on demand or at a fixed/determinable future time**
5. A **fixed sum** in money
6. To a **specified person or bearer**
Any condition attached to payment invalidates the bill. Administrative notes (e.g. 'keep receipt') are permissible.


---

## Bills of Exchange Act 1882 — s.3(2): What invalidates a bill #card
A bill is **not** a valid bill of exchange if payment is conditional on any event or further action. Examples:
- _"Pay if B delivers 100 bags of wheat"_ — invalid (conditional)
- _"Pay from my X account"_ — invalid (conditional on that account having funds)
The bill can only do one thing: order payment. It cannot attach any further condition or request.


---

## Bills of Exchange Act 1882 — s.3(4): Omissions that do NOT invalidate a bill #card
The following omissions leave the bill **still valid**:
- Missing **date**
- Missing statement of **value/consideration**
- Missing **place** of drawing or payment
These can be filled in by the holder (see s.20) or resolved by default rules in the Act.


---

## Bills of Exchange Act 1882 — s.20: Completing incomplete or irregular bills #card
The **holder** of an incomplete or irregular bill may complete it, provided:
- Done within a **reasonable time** (for cheques, ~6 months before the cheque becomes stale)
- Done **strictly within authority** (express or implied)
**Red line:** Signature cannot be filled in — a missing or forged signature is always fatal against the original drawer.
**HDC exception:** A holder in due course who takes the completed bill without notice can enforce it even if filled in beyond authority.


---

## Bills of Exchange Act 1882 — s.49: Notice of dishonour #card
If a bill is dishonoured (not paid or not accepted), the holder must **promptly notify** all endorsers and the drawer.
- **Effect of failure:** Endorsers are **discharged from liability** if timely notice is not given (drawers and acceptors remain liable regardless)
- **Form:** No strict form — oral, written, phone, or email all suffice
- **Purpose:** Allows prior parties to prepare for potential claims against them


---

## Bills of Exchange Act 1882 — s.64: Material alteration #card
If a bill's **essential terms** (amount, date, payee, drawee) are altered **without the assent of all liable parties**, the bill is **void**.
**Exceptions:**
- All parties **consent** — alteration valid
- Alteration is **not apparent** on the face — an HDC can still enforce the bill at its _original_ terms
Risk: a drawer could end up paying more or sooner than intended if an undetectable alteration is made and an HDC appears.


---

## Bills of Exchange Act 1882 — s.73: Definition of a cheque #card
- A cheque is a **bill of exchange drawn on a banker payable on demand**.
- Key distinction from a general bill of exchange: the **drawee must always be a bank**. All s.3 requirements apply. Governed additionally by s.81A (account payee cheques).


---

## Bills of Exchange Act 1882 — s.81A: Account payee cheques #card
Where a cheque is crossed and marked **'account payee'** (or A/C payee):
- The cheque is **not transferable**
- It can only be paid into the **account of the named payee**
- The bank must not pay it to anyone else
Useful where the drawer wants strict control over who receives payment. Limits both transferability and negotiability.


---

## Bills of Exchange Act 1882 — s.83: Promissory notes #card
A promissory note is an **unconditional promise in writing** by the maker to pay a fixed sum to a specified person or bearer.
**Key features:**
- Two parties only: **maker** (drawer) and **payee** — no drawee
- Date must be certain or determinable
- No conditions permitted
- Negotiable instrument
**Invalidity:** Conditional payment or uncertain date invalidates the note — see _Claydon v Bradley_: "by" a date rather than "on" a date = invalid.


---

## Sale of Goods Act 1979 — ss.2 & 28: Payment provisions #card
- **s.2(1):** A contract of sale involves the seller transferring (or agreeing to transfer) property in goods to the buyer for a **money consideration** (the price). Distinguishes sale from barter.
- **s.28:** Payment and delivery are **concurrent conditions** — unless otherwise agreed, the seller need not deliver until the buyer is ready to pay, and vice versa. Neither party can sue for non-performance if they themselves are not ready to perform.

## Coinage Act 1971 — Legal tender rules #card
Sets out what counts as **legal tender** in England and Wales and the limits per denomination:

| Denomination | Legal tender up to |
| ------------ | ------------------ |
| Gold coins   | Any amount         |
| £2 and £1    | Any amount         |
| 50p and 25p  | £10                |
| 10p and 5p   | £5                 |
| 1p and 2p    | 20p                |

**Effect:** If a debtor tenders legal tender and the creditor refuses (without having specified a different payment method), the debtor can use this as a **defence against non-payment claims**.
