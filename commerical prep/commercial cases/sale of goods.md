## s.2 Sale of Goods Act 1979 #card
- Contract of sale = seller transfers/agrees to transfer property in goods to buyer for money consideration (the price); distinguishes sale from agreement to sell.
    - Absolute or conditional
    - Property passes at contract = sale; passes later/on condition = agreement to sell → becomes sale when conditions met (s.2(6))
    - s.2(2): can exist between co-owners
    - Consideration must be money (not barter); part-exchange with money element qualifies
^1777401341025

## s.5 Sale of Goods Act 1979 #card
- Defines the subject matter of a contract of sale - existing vs future goods; specific vs unascertained goods.
    - Existing goods = owned/possessed by seller at contract
    - Future goods = to be manufactured/acquired after contract; purported present sale operates as agreement to sell only
    - s.5(2): contract valid even if seller's acquisition depends on a contingency
    - Specific goods = identified and agreed at contract (s.61)
    - Unascertained goods = defined by description only; not yet identified/separated 
^1777401341032

## s.6 & s.7 Sale of Goods Act 1979 #card
- s.6: contract void if specific goods have perished before contract, without seller's knowledge; s.7: agreement to sell avoided if specific goods perish after contract but before risk passes, without fault.
    - s.6: specific goods perished at time of contract (unknown to seller) → void
    - s.7: specific goods perish after agreement to sell, before risk passes, without fault → avoided
    - Both apply to specific goods only - not unascertained
    - "Perish" = physical destruction or commercially unusable (_Asfar v Blundell_ [1896])
    - Fault of either party prevents avoidance under s.7
^1777401341033

## s.11 Sale of Goods Act 1979 #card
- Governs when breach of condition is reduced to breach of warranty - once goods are accepted, the right to reject is lost.
    - s.11(2): buyer may waive condition or elect to treat breach as breach of warranty
    - s.11(3): condition vs warranty = construction of contract; label not determinative
    - s.11(4): contract not severable + buyer accepted goods → breach of condition treated as breach of warranty only; right to reject lost
    - s.11(4) subject to s.35A (partial rejection)
    - Not applicable to Scotland or CRA 2015 contracts
^1777401341034

## s.12 Sale of Goods Act 1979 #card
- Mandatory implied terms as to title - cannot be excluded, unlike ss.13-15.
    - s.12(1): implied condition - right to sell at time of sale (or at time property passes)
    - s.12(2)(a): implied warranty - goods free from undisclosed charges/encumbrances until property passes
    - s.12(2)(b): implied warranty of quiet possession
    - s.12(3)-(5): limited title contracts - reduced terms; still implied warranty of quiet possession against seller and those claiming through them
    - s.12(5A): s.12(1) = condition; ss.12(2)(4)(5) = warranties (E&W/NI)
    - Breach of s.12(1) = total failure of consideration → full price recoverable (_Rowland v Divall_ [1923])
    - Only truly mandatory implied term in the Act 
^1777401341035

## s.13 Sale of Goods Act 1979 #card
- Sale by description → implied condition goods correspond with description; buyer reliance = "natural index" of application (_Harlingdon & Leinster v Christopher Hull_ [1991]).
    - s.13(1): implied condition of correspondence with description
    - s.13(2): sale by sample and description → both must be satisfied
    - s.13(3): buyer selecting goods themselves does not prevent it being a sale by description
    - Description must be an essential term, not a passing label
    - Buyer reliance on description = objective test
    - Does not apply to CRA 2015 contracts 
^1777401341037

## s.14 Sale of Goods Act 1979 #card
- Implies satisfactory quality and fitness for purpose where seller sells in course of business; no implied quality terms in private sales (s.14(1)).
    - s.14(2): implied condition of satisfactory quality - standard a reasonable person would regard as satisfactory (description, price, circumstances)
    - s.14(2A): reasonable person test
    - s.14(2B): quality includes fitness for common purposes, appearance/finish, freedom from minor defects, safety, durability
    - s.14(2C): no condition re defects drawn to buyer's attention, revealed by examination, or apparent on examination of sample
    - s.14(3): implied condition of fitness for particular purpose communicated to seller, unless reliance unreasonable
    - s.14(4): quality terms may be annexed by trade usage
    - s.14(5): applies to agents selling in course of business (unless principal not in business + buyer knows/is told)
    - s.14(6): ss.14(2) and (3) are conditions (E&W/NI)
^1777401341038

## s.15 Sale of Goods Act 1979 #card
- Sale by sample → implied conditions that bulk matches sample in quality and goods are free from defects not apparent on reasonable examination of sample.
    - s.15(1): sale by sample = express or implied term to that effect
    - s.15(2)(a): bulk must correspond with sample in quality
    - s.15(2)(c): goods free from defect making them unsatisfactory not apparent on reasonable examination of sample
    - s.15(3): implied term is a condition (E&W/NI)
    - Latent defects not detectable on examination of sample remain covered
    - Does not apply to CRA 2015 contracts 
^1777401341039

## s.15A Sale of Goods Act 1979 #card
- In non-consumer cases, breach of ss.13/14/15 so slight that rejection would be unreasonable → treated as breach of warranty only, not condition.
    - s.15A(1): applies where non-consumer buyer would otherwise have right to reject but breach is slight → treated as warranty breach
    - s.15A(2): displaced by contrary contractual intention
    - s.15A(3): burden on seller to show breach was slight
    - Does not apply to consumer contracts or Scotland
    - Introduced by SSGA 1994 to prevent rejection for trivial defects in commercial dealings 
^1777401341040

## s.16 Sale of Goods Act 1979 #card
- No property in unascertained goods passes until ascertained - absolute precondition; subject to s.20A exception for bulk goods.
    - Property cannot pass in unascertained goods until ascertainment - cannot be contracted around
    - Ascertained = identified after contract (distinct from specific goods identified at contract)
    - s.20A: undivided share in identified bulk may pass to pre-paying buyer before physical separation
    - Applies even where parties intend earlier passing 
^1777401341041

## s.17 Sale of Goods Act 1979 #card
- Property in specific/ascertained goods passes when parties intend - s.17 is primary; s.18 is fallback only.
    - s.17(1): property passes when parties intend
    - s.17(2): intention = contract terms + conduct + circumstances
    - s.18 rules apply only if s.17 cannot resolve the question
    - Retention of title (Romalpa) clauses defer passing of property
    - Express or implied intention; commercial context and trade custom relevant 
^1777401341042

## s.18 Sale of Goods Act 1979 #card
- Five default rules for ascertaining when property passes, applicable only where s.17 fails to resolve intention.
    - Rule 1: unconditional contract, specific goods, deliverable state → passes at contract (_Tarling v Baxter_)
    - Rule 2: seller must do something to put goods in deliverable state → passes when done + buyer notified
    - Rule 3: weighing/measuring/testing to fix price → passes when done + buyer notified
    - Rule 4: sale or return → passes on approval/adoption or lapse of reasonable time without rejection
    - Rule 5: unascertained/future goods → unconditional appropriation + assent of other party
    - Rule 5(3)-(4): bulk reduced to/below aggregate quantity due to single buyer → property passes automatically
    - "Deliverable state" = buyer would be bound to take delivery under contract; s.61(5) statutory definition (_Underwood v Burgh Castle_ [1922]) 
^1777401341043

## s.20 Sale of Goods Act 1979 #card
- Risk follows property (_res perit domino_) by default - seller bears risk until property passes; buyer bears risk thereafter, regardless of delivery.
    - s.20(1): risk passes with property, whether or not delivery made
    - s.20(2): delivery delayed by fault of either party → risk on the party at fault for loss that might not have occurred but for the fault
    - s.20(3): does not affect bailee duties/liabilities
    - s.20(4): does not apply to CRA 2015 contracts
    - Parties may contract out (e.g. "goods travel at seller's risk")
    - Risk passing separately from property common in commercial contracts (FOB, CIF) 
^1777401341044

## s.21 Sale of Goods Act 1979 #card
- Nemo dat quod non habet - seller cannot pass better title than they have; exceptions preserve innocent third-party purchasers.
    - s.21(1): buyer acquires no better title than seller had, unless owner estopped from denying seller's authority
    - Estoppel: owner by conduct precluded from denying seller's authority
    - s.21(2)(a): Factors Acts preserved - mercantile agent in possession with consent can pass good title to bona fide purchaser for value
    - s.21(2)(b): sales under court order or special statutory power preserved
    - Further exceptions: s.23 (voidable title), s.24 (seller in possession), s.25 (buyer in possession) 
^1777401341045

## s.23 Sale of Goods Act 1979 #card
- Exception to nemo dat - buyer from seller with voidable (not yet avoided) title acquires good title if bona fide purchaser without notice.
    - Seller's title must be voidable (e.g. fraud/misrepresentation), not void - void title cannot be passed at all
    - Buyer must purchase in good faith and without notice of defect
    - Owner must avoid contract before sub-sale to bona fide purchaser - delay defeats right (_Car and Universal Finance v Caldwell_ [1965])
    - Once bona fide purchaser acquires title under s.23, owner's right of rescission destroyed
    - Good faith = honesty in fact (s.61(3)) 
^1777401341046

## s.24 Sale of Goods Act 1979 #card
- Exception to nemo dat - seller remaining in possession after sale can pass good title to bona fide third party purchaser without notice.
    - Seller has sold goods but remains in possession (or holds documents of title)
    - Further delivery/transfer under sale, pledge or other disposition to good-faith buyer without notice = same effect as if seller were authorised by owner
    - Applies to mercantile agents acting for seller in possession
    - Possession must be physical or through documents of title
    - Protects commercial expectation that possession = authority to deal 
^1777401341047

## s.25 Sale of Goods Act 1979 #card
- Exception to nemo dat - buyer in possession with seller's consent can pass good title to bona fide third party purchaser without notice.
    - Person who has bought/agreed to buy + obtained possession with seller's consent → further disposition to good-faith buyer without notice has same effect as if made by mercantile agent with owner's consent
    - s.25(2)(a): buyer under conditional sale agreement (within CCA 1974) excluded - property has not passed
    - Applies to physical goods and documents of title
    - Recipient must take in good faith and without notice of any lien/right of original seller
    - Differs from s.24 - applies where buyer (not seller) has possession 
^1777401341048

## s.28 & s.29 Sale of Goods Act 1979 #card
- Delivery and payment are concurrent conditions by default; s.29 sets default rules for place, time and manner of delivery.
    - s.28: delivery and payment concurrent - seller ready and willing to deliver in exchange for price, and vice versa
    - s.29(1): buyer to collect or seller to send = depends on contract
    - s.29(2): default place = seller's place of business/residence; specific goods known to be elsewhere → that place
    - s.29(3): seller bound to send, no time fixed → must send within reasonable time
    - s.29(4): goods held by third party → no delivery until third party acknowledges to buyer
    - s.29(5): demand/tender at unreasonable hour may be treated as ineffectual
    - s.29(6): expenses of putting goods in deliverable state borne by seller unless agreed otherwise 
^1777401341049

## s.30 Sale of Goods Act 1979 #card
- Wrong quantity delivered - under-delivery and over-delivery both give rise to rights of rejection.
    - s.30(1): under-delivery → buyer may reject; or accept and pay at contract rate
    - s.30(2): over-delivery → accept contract quantity + reject excess; or reject whole
    - s.30(2A): non-consumer - may not reject if shortfall/excess so slight rejection would be unreasonable
    - s.30(2B): burden on seller to show shortfall/excess was slight
    - s.30(3): buyer accepts whole over-delivery → must pay at contract rate for excess
    - s.30(5): subject to trade usage, special agreement or course of dealing
    - Does not apply to CRA 2015 contracts 
^1777401341050

## s.35 Sale of Goods Act 1979 #card
- Acceptance bars right to reject and treat as repudiated - buyer then limited to damages claim.
    - Acceptance when: (a) intimates acceptance; (b) act inconsistent with seller's ownership; (c) retains beyond reasonable time without rejection
    - s.34: buyer must have had reasonable opportunity to examine before acceptance runs
    - s.35(4): reasonable time = question of fact
    - s.35(6)(a): requesting/agreeing to repair ≠ acceptance (_Clegg v Andersson_ [2003])
    - s.35(6)(b): examining goods ≠ acceptance
    - s.35(7): goods delivered to sub-buyer, no reasonable opportunity to examine → time not run
    - Amended by SSGA 1994 to strengthen rejection rights 
^1777401341051

## s.39 Sale of Goods Act 1979 #card
- Unpaid seller's rights against the goods - subsist even where property has passed to buyer.
    - s.38(1): unpaid = whole price not paid/tendered; or negotiable instrument given as conditional payment dishonoured
    - s.38(2): "seller" includes agents, consignors, those who have indorsed a bill of lading
    - s.39(1)(a): lien - retain goods for price while in possession
    - s.39(1)(b): stoppage in transit - on buyer's insolvency
    - s.39(1)(c): right of resale (as limited by s.48)
    - s.39(2): property not passed → right to withhold delivery co-extensive with above
    - Lien is possessory - lost on voluntary parting with possession (s.43) 
^1777401341052

## s.49 Sale of Goods Act 1979 #card
- Seller's action for price as debt - requires property to have passed or price payable on day certain; no proof of loss needed.
    - s.49(1): property passed + buyer wrongfully refuses to pay → action for price
    - s.49(2): price payable on day certain → action for price even if property not passed and goods not appropriated
    - Neither condition met → confined to damages under s.50
    - s.49 generally exhaustive - no general discretion to award price (_PST Energy v OW Bunker_ [2016])
    - No proof of loss required (contrast s.50) 
^1777401341053

## s.50 Sale of Goods Act 1979 #card
- Seller's damages for non-acceptance - where buyer wrongfully refuses to accept and pay and s.49 unavailable.
    - s.50(1): action for damages for non-acceptance
    - s.50(2): measure = estimated loss directly and naturally resulting from breach
    - s.50(3): available market → prima facie measure = contract price minus market price at time goods ought to have been accepted
    - Market price = contract price → nominal damages only
    - "Available market" = market where goods of that description can readily be bought and sold (_Thompson v Robinson_ [1955]) 
^1777401341054

## s.51 Sale of Goods Act 1979 #card
- Buyer's damages for non-delivery - where seller wrongfully refuses to deliver.
    - s.51(1): action for damages for non-delivery
    - s.51(2): measure = estimated loss directly and naturally resulting from breach
    - s.51(3): available market → prima facie measure = market price at time of delivery minus contract price
    - Mirror of s.50 from buyer's perspective
    - Does not apply to CRA 2015 contracts
    - Buyer must mitigate - failure to buy in available market reduces recovery
^1777401341055

## s.53 Sale of Goods Act 1979 #card
- Buyer's remedy for breach of warranty - cannot reject but may set off against price or claim damages.
    - s.53(1): breach of warranty → buyer cannot reject; may (a) set up breach in diminution/extinction of price; or (b) claim damages
    - s.53(2): measure = estimated loss directly and naturally resulting from breach
    - s.53(3): breach of warranty of quality → prima facie measure = value of goods at delivery vs value if warranted
    - s.53(4): using breach as price defence does not bar further damages claim for additional loss
    - Buyer elects to treat condition as warranty (s.11) → accepts goods but retains right to damages
^1777401341056

## s.61 Sale of Goods Act 1979 #card
- Interpretation section - statutory definitions of key terms throughout the Act.
    - "Goods" = all personal chattels other than things in action and money; includes emblements, industrial growing crops, things attached to land agreed to be severed; includes undivided share in goods
    - "Property" = general property in goods, not merely special property (full ownership, not lien/pledge)
    - "Delivery" = voluntary transfer of possession from one person to another
    - "Specific goods" = identified and agreed at time of contract; includes undivided share as fraction/percentage of identified goods
    - "Future goods" = to be manufactured or acquired by seller after contract
    - "Warranty" (E&W/NI) = collateral term; breach → damages only, not right to reject
    - s.61(3): good faith = honestly, whether or not negligently
    - s.61(5): deliverable state = buyer would be bound to take delivery under the contract 
^1777401341057

## Clegg v. Olle Andersson (t/a Nordic Marine) [2003] EWCA Civ 320 #card
- new yacht with a shoal draught keel
- on delivery, keel 607kg overweight
- s.14(2) - more than minimal remedial work did not meet the standard a reasonable person would regard as satisfactory; even if the cost of repair was relatively low
- time for acceptance does not run while the buyer is still awaiting information promised by the seller necessary to make an informed decision. Asking for or agreeing to repair does not constitute acceptance (s.35(6)(a)). ^1777401341058

## Underwood Ltd v. Burgh Castle Brick & Cement Syndicate [1922] 1 KB 343 #card
- agreement to sell engine fixed by bolts embedded in concrete floor
- whole being unloaded, the concrete floor was broken
- buyers refused to accept it
- what are intentions of parties?
- s.18 r.1 deliverable state - buyer would be bound to take delivery under
- s.18 r.2 putting into a deliverable state - property does not pass until that is done and the buyer has notice. ^1777401341059

## Philip Head & Sons Ltd v. Showfronts Ltd [1970] 1 Lloyd's Rep 140 #card
- carpet to be laid
- carpet stolen
- argued property had not passed
- s.18 r.5 - not in a deliverable state (layed)
- "deliverable state" is assessed by reference to the contractual obligation to accept, not by the physical existence of the goods. ^1777401341060

## Healy v. Howlett & Sons [1917] 1 KB 337 #card
- 20 boxes of hard, bright mackerel
- train was delayed; fish missed the boat to Holyhead and arrived in London in an unmerchantable condition
- An invoice bearing the words "at sole risk of purchaser after putting fish on rail here" was sent after the contract was concluded
- s.18 r.5
    - property of unascertained passes upon unconditional aproproation
    - 190 boxes of fish cosigned for seperate buyers
    - 20 boxes not clearly identified
    - Property in unascertained goods passes only upon unconditional appropriation of specific goods to the contract with the assent of the other party.
        - Where 190 boxes were consigned for three separate buyers with no identification of which 20 belonged to the defendants at the point of loading, there was no appropriation at Valentia and so property did not pass there. 
^1777401341061

## Carlos Federspiel & Co SA v. Charles Twigg & Co Ltd [1957] 1 Lloyd's Rep 240 #card
- bicycles paid for in advance
- crates packed and marked with buyers name
- sellers debenture holders refused to deliver
- buyers argued that property had already passed
- Under an FOB contract, property passes on shipment, not on prior packing or marking.
- seller can substitute or redirect goods until the point of actual shipment on an FOB contract; prep steps do not appropriate goods to the contract. 
^1777401341062
