
## Foley v Hill (1848) 2 HLC 28  #card
- A customer deposited money with a bank at interest. 
- The customer later sought to recover the money as a trust fund rather than a simple debt.
- Money paid into a bank account belongs legally to the bank, not the customer. 
- The relationship between bank and customer is one of debtor and creditor

## Joachimson v Swiss Bank Corporation [1921] 3 KB 110  #card
- A German national held a bank account with Swiss Bank Corporation in England.
- During WWI, trading with the enemy legislation meant demand for repayment could not be made. 
- The bank–customer relationship is a debtor–creditor relationship. The bank's obligation to repay money in a current account only arises when the customer makes a **formal demand** for payment.

## Tournier v National Provincial and Union Bank of England [1924] 1 KB 461  #card
- The bank disclosed information about Tournier's account (including that he was paying a bookmaker) to his employer, causing him to lose his job. 
- Tournier sued the bank for breach of confidence.
- A bank owes its customer an implied duty of **confidentiality** regarding information obtained through the banking relationship. 
- This duty has four exceptions permitting disclosure: 
- (1) where the law **compels** it; 
- (2) where there is a **public duty** to disclose; 
- (3) where the **bank's interests** require it; and 
- (4) where the customer has given **express or implied consent**.

## Lipkin Gorman v Karpnale & Co [1989] 1 WLR 1340 (CA) / [1991] 2 AC 548 (HL)  #card
- A partner at a firm of solicitors stole client money from the firm's account and gambled it away at a casino (Karpnale). 
- The solicitors' firm sued the casino to recover the money.
- Money paid under a mistake or without authority may be recoverable in **unjust enrichment** (formerly "money had and received").

## London Joint Stock Bank Ltd v Macmillan [1918] AC 777  #card
- A clerk presented a cheque to Macmillan (his employer) for signature.
- The cheque was drawn for £2 in figures but the words were left blank.
- The clerk later altered the figures to £120 and filled in the words to match.
- The bank paid £120 and was sued by the customer.
- A customer owes a duty to their bank to **draw cheques with reasonable care** so as not to facilitate fraud or alteration.

## Marfani & Co Ltd v Midland Bank Ltd [1968] 1 WLR 956  #card
-  A fraudster opened a bank account using a stolen reference and then paid in a cheque belonging to Marfani, obtaining payment. 
- Marfani sued Midland Bank for conversion of the cheque.
- A bank that collects payment of a cheque for a customer is protected from liability for **conversion** under s.4 of the Cheques Act 1957, provided it acts in **good faith and without negligence**. 
- The test for negligence is whether the bank acted as a reasonable banker would in the circumstances

## United Dominions Trust Ltd v Kirkwood [1966] 2 QB 431  #card
- UDT sought to rely on the statutory protection available to "bankers" under the Bills of Exchange Act. 
- The question was whether UDT, a finance company, qualified as a "banker."
- Sets out the characteristics of a **banker** in English law:
- (1) accepting deposits of money from customers; 
- (2) honouring cheques drawn on the banker by customers; and
- (3) collecting cheques for customers.

## Woods v Martins Bank Ltd [1959] 1 QB 55 #card
- Woods, a customer of Martins Bank, alleged that the bank manager gave him negligent advice regarding an investment, causing him loss.
- The bank–customer relationship may extend beyond the mere debtor–creditor relationship. 
- Where a bank undertakes to give advice or guidance to a customer, it owes a **duty of care** in giving that advice.

## Great Western Railway Co v London and County Banking Co [1901] AC 414  #card
- A fraudster obtained cheques payable to "the order of" the GWR by forging indorsements. 
- The bank collected the cheques for the fraudster and paid the proceeds to him.
- GWR sued the bank for conversion.
- A bank collecting a cheque on behalf of a customer may be liable for **conversion** if the customer had no title to the cheque.

## Stoney Stanton Supplies (Coventry) Ltd v Midland Bank Ltd [1966] 2 Lloyd's Rep 373  #card
- A company's account was overdrawn.
- A director drew a cheque in the company's favour on another account he controlled and paid it into the overdrawn account. 
- The bank applied the funds to reduce the overdraft rather than crediting the account for the company's use.
- A bank is entitled to exercise its **right of set-off** (banker's set-off or combination of accounts) to apply a credit balance in one account against a debit balance in another
- provided the accounts are held by the same customer in the same capacity. 
- The bank does not require the customer's consent to do so. This right is a fundamental incident of the banker–customer relationship.

## Matthews v Brown & Co (1984) (The Times, 23 March 1984) #card
- A cheque was post-dated and presented to the bank for payment before the date written on its face. 
- The bank paid it.
- A post-dated cheque is valid as a bill of exchange but the bank should not pay it before the date stated on the cheque.
- If a bank pays a post-dated cheque before its date, it does so at its own risk

## Turner v Royal Bank of Scotland plc [1999] 2 All ER (Comm) 664 #card
- Turner opened a bank account. When he later applied for credit elsewhere, RBS provided a bankers' reference (status inquiry) about him that was unfavourable, without his knowledge. T
- urner argued the bank had no authority to disclose the information.
- A bank may only provide a **bankers' reference** about a customer with the customer's **consent** — either express or implied from the course of dealing or the general practice of the banking industry.

## First City Monument Bank Plc v Zumax Nigeria Ltd [2019] EWCA Civ 294 #card
- Zumax paid money to First City Monument Bank for a specific purpose (onward transmission). 
- A dispute arose as to whether the bank held those funds on trust for Zumax or whether the money simply became the bank's own as a deposit.
- Confirms that money paid into a bank account ordinarily becomes the property of the bank

## Commissioners of Taxation v English, Scottish and Australian Bank [1920] AC 683 #card
- A cheque payable to the Commissioners of Taxation was stolen and paid into an account at the English, Scottish and Australian Bank by the thief, who obtained payment.
- The Commissioners sued the collecting bank.
- A **collecting bank** that receives payment of a cheque for a customer in good faith and without negligence is protected from liability in conversion. 
- The bank is also entitled to the protection of being a **holder in due course** where it takes a cheque for value, in good faith, without notice of defect.

## Westminster Bank Ltd v Hilton (1926) 43 TLR 124 #card
- A customer gave a bank a standing order to pay a third party on a particular date. 
- Due to an administrative error, the bank paid the order on the wrong date 
- when the customer had insufficient funds, causing the payment to bounce on the correct date.
- A bank must follow its customer's payment instructions **precisely and strictly**.

## Price Waterhouse v BCCI Holdings (Luxembourg) SA [1992] BCLC 583 #card
- Price Waterhouse, auditors of BCCI, were sought to be compelled to disclose confidential information about BCCI to regulators and third parties following BCCI's collapse.
- The duty of confidentiality owed by a bank (and those dealing with confidential banking information, such as auditors) to its customers is not absolute. 
- Disclosure is permissible — and may be required — where there is a **public interest** in disclosure, particularly in cases involving serious fraud or regulatory investigation.
