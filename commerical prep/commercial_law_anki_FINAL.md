=== AGENCY ===

Agency --- purpose Makes a contract enforceable between a PRINCIPAL and
a THIRD PARTY through an agent, circumventing the doctrine of privity
(Dunlop v Selfridges \[1915\])

Authority taxonomy --- Freeman & Lockyer v Buckhurst Park \[1964\]
(Diplock LJ) (1) Express actual authority --- expressly granted; (2)
Implied actual authority --- from nature of role/relationship; (3)
Apparent/ostensible authority --- from principal\'s representation to TP

Creation by agreement --- three forms (1) Express --- normal contract
rules, no formalities required (Heard v Pilley); (2) Implied --- from
nature of job/relationship (Waugh v HB Clifford & Sons); (3) Usual ---
through trade custom (Watteau v Fenwick)

Usual authority --- the rule (Watteau v Fenwick) Principal liable for
ALL acts of the agent within the authority USUALLY CONFIDED to agents of
that character, even if principal placed limitations on that authority

Usual authority --- must be (Graves v Legg; Perry v Barnett) NOTORIOUS,
CERTAIN and REASONABLE

Ratification --- THREE pre-conditions (1) Agent must purport to act for
a NAMED, IDENTIFIABLE principal (Keighley, Maxstead v Durant); (2)
Principal must have EXISTED and been ASCERTAINABLE at time of contract
(Kelner v Baxter); (3) Principal must have had CAPACITY (Firth v
Staines)

Ratification --- four validity requirements (1) TIMELY (Re Portuguese
Consolidated Copper Mines); (2) Not ratifying a NULLITY (Danish
Mercantile v Beaumont); (3) With FULL KNOWLEDGE of facts; (4) By words
or conduct --- The Kilmun (no need to communicate to TP)

Ratification --- Bolton Partners v Lambert Once principal ratifies, TP
is BOUND even if they withdrew their offer BEFORE ratification occurred

Ratification --- undisclosed principal An undisclosed principal CANNOT
be ratified

Apparent authority --- FIVE requirements (1) Representation that person
had authority (Summers v Solomon); (2) Representation of FACT not law
(Kleinwort Benson v Lincoln CC); (3) Made by PRINCIPAL to TP (The Ocean
Frost; Dickson v Valpy); (4) TP RELIED on it (The Ocean Frost; Jacobs v
Morris); (5) ALTERNATIVE: principal estopped by subsequent conduct
(Spiro v Lintern)

Apparent authority --- vitiation Vitiated by forgery if principal held
out forger as having authority --- Uxbridge Permanent Benefit Building
Society v Pickard

Agency by necessity --- THREE requirements (1) Only PRACTICABLE action
available (Couturier v Hastie; Prager v Blatspiel); (2) No opportunity
to get INSTRUCTIONS (Springer v GWR); (3) Acted HONESTLY and in the
owner\'s interests

Disclosed principal TP knows agent acts for a principal → principal and
TP BOUND; agent NOT normally personally liable

Undisclosed principal TP does not know of principal → agent MAY BE SUED
on contract; principal cannot be ratified

Agent\'s common law duties (1) Obey lawful instructions; (2) Exercise
reasonable care and skill; (3) Act personally --- no sub-delegation
without authority

Agent\'s fiduciary duties (1) No conflict of interest; (2) No secret
profit; (3) Account for ALL profits from position; (4) Knowledge
acquired = fiduciary asset --- must account for profits from it. Even
self-appointed agents have same fiduciary duties.

Principal\'s common law duties (1) Pay agreed remuneration/commission;
(2) Indemnify agent against losses (Adams v Morgan; Barron v
Fitzgerald); (3) Not prevent agent earning commission. Indemnity lost
if: transaction illegal; agency illegal; agent in breach.

Commercial Agents Regulations 1993 --- definition (Reg 2) Self-employed
intermediary with CONTINUING AUTHORITY to negotiate/conclude the SALE OR
PURCHASE OF GOODS. Applies to GOODS ONLY --- not services. Retained EU
law under s.25 EU (Withdrawal Agreement) Act 2020.

Commercial Agents Regulations --- exclusions (Reg 2.1--2.3) (1) Internal
parties --- company officers, partners; (2) Commodity exchanges/markets
(W Nagel v Pluczenik --- DeBeers \'sights\' = commodity market → Regs
did NOT apply); (3) Gratuitous agents --- must be for remuneration; (4)
Ancillary/secondary activities (Crane v Sky In-Home Service --- agency
not primary function); (5) Distributors --- buy/resell on own behalf

Commercial Agents Regulations --- good faith duty (Regs 3 & 4) Regs
introduce GOOD FAITH requirement into English commercial law. Agent:
look after principal\'s interests, act dutifully in good faith, make
proper efforts to negotiate, keep principal informed, comply with
reasonable instructions. Principal: act in good faith, provide necessary
docs/info, notify agent of anticipated volume drop, timely notification
of acceptance/rejection of transactions.

Commercial Agents Regulations --- most duties (Reg 5) CANNOT be excluded
by contract --- mandatory

Commercial Agents Regulations --- commission timing (Reg 10(3)) Paid no
later than LAST DAY OF MONTH FOLLOWING THE QUARTER in which it became
due. With a statement of commission including main components. Extracts
from books to be supplied on demand.

Commercial Agents Regulations --- notice periods (Reg 15) 1 MONTH ---
1st year; 2 MONTHS --- 2nd year; 3 MONTHS --- 3rd year and beyond.
Fixed-term continuing after expiry = automatically INDEFINITE (Reg 14).
Provision allowing principal shorter notice than agent = VOID.

Commercial Agents Regulations --- immediate termination (Reg 16)
Permitted ONLY for failure to perform contractual obligations OR
exceptional circumstances

Commercial Agents Regulations --- post-termination compensation (Reg 17)
DEFAULT. Reflects LOSS OF FUTURE COMMISSION. Cap: UNLIMITED. Lonsdale v
Howard & Hallam \[2007\]: UK rejected French 2-year rule → compensation
= MARKET VALUE of agency at date of termination (what hypothetical
purchaser would pay for goodwill). Failing business = very low
compensation.

Commercial Agents Regulations --- post-termination indemnity (Reg 17)
Must be agreed in WRITING. Rewards GOODWILL handed over. Cap: 1 YEAR\'S
AVERAGE ANNUAL COMMISSION. Agent must show new customers brought or
business significantly increased.

Commercial Agents Regulations --- restraint of trade (Reg 20) ALL FOUR
required: (1) In WRITING; (2) Same GEOGRAPHICAL AREA or customer group;
(3) Same KIND OF GOODS; (4) Maximum TWO YEARS duration

Termination --- consensual grounds Performance (Blackburn v Scholes);
fixed term expires (Dickinson v Lilwal); notice (Decro-Wall ---
effective even if breach); inconsistent conduct (Nelson v Rolfe);
insanity (Yonge v Toynbee); death; dissolution; bankruptcy;
cessation/transfer of business (Rhodes v Forward; Turner v Goldsmith)

Termination --- non-terminable (irrevocable) agencies (1) Agency coupled
with INDEPENDENT INTEREST (not arising from the agency itself; created
for value or by deed); (2) Agent has become PERSONALLY LIABLE; (3)
Irrevocable/enduring POWER OF ATTORNEY

=== SALE OF GOODS ===

SGA 1979 --- definition of \'goods\' (s.61) All personal chattels OTHER
THAN things in action and money. Includes industrial growing crops;
items to be severed from land before sale; collectors\' coins. EXCLUDES
shares, court judgments, money in ordinary use.

SGA 1979 --- \'sale\' (s.2(1)) Transfer of property in goods from seller
to buyer for a MONEY CONSIDERATION (the price)

SGA 1979 --- \'sale\' vs \'agreement to sell\' (s.2(4)/(5)) Sale
(s.2(4)): property transferred NOW. Agreement to sell (s.2(5)): transfer
to take place in FUTURE or on condition.

SGA 1979 --- perished goods s.6: goods perished AT TIME of contract →
contract VOID. s.7: goods perish AFTER agreement but BEFORE property
passes → agreement AVOIDED.

SGA 1979 --- seller\'s duties s.27: duty to deliver; s.28: payment and
delivery are CONCURRENT CONDITIONS; s.29: place/time of delivery; s.30:
wrong quantity rules; s.31: instalment deliveries

SGA 1979 --- time stipulations (s.10) Time of DELIVERY is of the
ESSENCE. Time of PAYMENT is NOT of the essence.

SGA 1979 --- s.12 (title) Implied CONDITION: seller has right to sell.
Implied WARRANTY: quiet possession; freedom from undisclosed
encumbrances.

SGA 1979 --- s.13 (description) Goods must CORRESPOND WITH DESCRIPTION.
Applies even if buyer saw goods (Beale v Taylor). Applies even when
goods displayed on counter (Grant v AKM). Applies where buyer has not
seen goods and relies on description (Varley v Whipp). Breach of s.13 =
breach of CONDITION.

SGA 1979 --- s.14(2) (satisfactory quality) BUSINESS SELLER ONLY.
Satisfactory quality as a REASONABLE PERSON would consider. s.14(2B):
fitness for ALL common purposes; appearance/finish; freedom from minor
defects; safety; durability. Latent defects COVERED even if undetectable
on examination (Grant v AKM).

SGA 1979 --- s.14(3) (fitness for purpose) BUSINESS SELLER ONLY. Buyer
makes known PARTICULAR PURPOSE (expressly or impliedly --- reliance
inferred from act of buying, Grant v AKM). Goods must be REASONABLY FIT
for that purpose.

SGA 1979 --- s.15 (sale by sample) Bulk must CORRESPOND WITH SAMPLE.
Free from defects making quality unsatisfactory not apparent on
REASONABLE EXAMINATION of sample (s.15(2)(c)).

SGA 1979 --- s.15A (non-consumer modification) In NON-CONSUMER
contracts, if breach of ss.13--15 is so SLIGHT that rejection would be
unreasonable → breach treated as WARRANTY only (damages, not rejection).
Does NOT apply to consumer sales.

SGA 1979 --- s.16 (unascertained goods) Property CANNOT pass until goods
are ASCERTAINED. s.20A: buyer of specified quantity from identified bulk
acquires UNDIVIDED SHARE proportional to quantity paid for.

SGA 1979 --- s.17 (intention governs property) Property passes when
parties INTEND it to pass. Intention: construe contract, correspondence,
conduct, context. If unclear → s.18 applies.

SGA 1979 --- s.18 Rule 1 Unconditional contract for SPECIFIC GOODS in
DELIVERABLE STATE → property passes when CONTRACT MADE. \'Deliverable
state\' = buyer bound to take delivery. Clegg v Olle Andersson;
Underwood v Burgh Castle (engine in concrete NOT deliverable).

SGA 1979 --- s.18 Rule 2 Specific goods where seller must do something
to put them in DELIVERABLE STATE → property passes when act DONE and
buyer NOTIFIED. Underwood v Burgh Castle; Philip Head v Showfronts
(carpet in bales NOT deliverable).

SGA 1979 --- s.18 Rule 3 Specific goods to be WEIGHED/MEASURED/TESTED to
ascertain price → passes when done and buyer NOTIFIED.

SGA 1979 --- s.18 Rule 4 Goods on SALE OR RETURN → property passes when
buyer: (a) SIGNIFIES APPROVAL; OR (b) retains beyond REASONABLE TIME
without rejection.

SGA 1979 --- s.18 Rule 5 UNASCERTAINED/FUTURE goods → property passes
when goods UNCONDITIONALLY APPROPRIATED to contract with ASSENT of other
party. Assent may be prior or subsequent. Carlos Federspiel v Twigg
\[1957\]: packing/labelling/booking freight NOT unconditional
appropriation on FOB --- property passes on SHIPMENT.

SGA 1979 --- s.19 (title retention) Seller may RESERVE RIGHT OF DISPOSAL
--- retains property until condition fulfilled. Very common B2B
retention of title/Romalpa clause.

SGA 1979 --- s.20 (risk) Default: goods at SELLER\'S risk until property
passes; at BUYER\'S risk once property passes --- whether or not
delivered. Fault exception (s.20(2)): if delay caused by FAULT of either
party, goods at risk of the PARTY AT FAULT (as to losses that would not
otherwise have occurred). Freedom of contract --- parties may agree
otherwise.

SGA 1979 --- s.35 (acceptance) Accepted if: (a) INTIMATES acceptance to
seller; (b) does ACT INCONSISTENT with seller\'s ownership; (c) retains
beyond REASONABLE TIME. s.35(4): reasonable time = QUESTION OF FACT.
s.35(6): does NOT constitute acceptance: asking for/agreeing to REPAIR;
EXAMINING goods. Time does not run while buyer awaiting information
needed to make informed decision --- Clegg v Olle Andersson.

SGA 1979 --- s.11(4) (effect of acceptance) Once ACCEPTED, breach of
condition treated as BREACH OF WARRANTY only --- damages but NOT
rejection.

SGA 1979 --- s.35A (partial rejection) Buyer may ACCEPT conforming goods
and REJECT non-conforming goods --- right of partial rejection.

SGA 1979 --- nemo dat rule (s.21) Cannot pass BETTER TITLE than you have
--- nemo dat quod non habet.

SGA 1979 --- s.21(1) estoppel exception Owner\'s conduct leads buyer to
believe seller had authority → owner ESTOPPED from denying title

SGA 1979 --- s.23 exception (voidable title) Sale under VOIDABLE TITLE
to bona fide purchaser for value WITHOUT NOTICE before title avoided →
good title passes

SGA 1979 --- s.24 exception (seller in possession) Seller continues in
possession AFTER sale; delivers to 2nd good faith buyer → 2nd buyer gets
GOOD TITLE

SGA 1979 --- s.25 exception (buyer in possession) Buyer obtains
possession BEFORE property passes; transfers to good faith TP → TP gets
GOOD TITLE

SGA 1979 --- unpaid seller\'s three rights (s.39) (1) LIEN (s.41):
retain goods until paid; (2) STOPPAGE IN TRANSIT (s.44): stop goods in
transit if buyer insolvent; (3) RESALE (s.48): resell if buyer in
default or perishables

SGA 1979 --- s.49 (action for price) s.49(1): property HAS PASSED +
buyer wrongfully refuses to pay → action for PRICE. s.49(2): price
payable on a GIVEN DAY → action for price regardless of property.

SGA 1979 --- s.50 (damages for non-acceptance) Measure: difference
between CONTRACT PRICE and MARKET PRICE at time goods ought to have been
accepted.

SGA 1979 --- s.51 (damages for non-delivery) Measure: difference between
CONTRACT PRICE and MARKET PRICE at time goods ought to have been
delivered.

Carlos Federspiel v Charles Twigg \[1957\] Rule 5 appropriation on FOB
contracts: packing, labelling, booking freight space = preparatory acts,
NOT unconditional appropriation. Property passes on ACTUAL SHIPMENT.
Important on seller\'s insolvency --- buyer who paid in advance = only
unsecured creditor if goods not shipped.

Varley v Whipp \[1900\] s.13 sale by description applies where buyer has
NOT seen goods. Breach = breach of CONDITION (right to reject). Property
cannot pass if s.13 breached and buyer never accepts.

Grant v Australian Knitting Mills \[1936\] s.14(2) --- latent defect
(sulphites in underwear) = breach even if undetectable. Reliance on
retailer inferred. Sale by description can apply even when goods on
display counter. Defect easily cured is still a breach.

Clegg v Olle Andersson \[2003\] s.14(2) --- low repair cost not
determinative of satisfactory quality. s.35 --- time for acceptance
doesn\'t run while buyer awaiting seller\'s information about defect.
Agreeing to repair ≠ acceptance.

=== BAILMENTS ===

Bailment --- nature A SEPARATE FORM OF OBLIGATION (alongside contract,
tort, restitution). Created by contract, consent, or construction
(including non-consensually e.g. theft). All contractual bailments are
consensual; not all consensual bailments are contractual.

Bailment --- what can be bailed Only PERSONAL, TANGIBLE property. NOT
land; NOT choses in action. Valuable PAPER can be bailed (share
certificates, insurance docs, cash).

Bailment --- types (Coggs v Bernard) Depositum (keep without fee);
Commodatum (lend for use without charge); Locatio et conductio (hire for
reward); Vadium (pledge); Locatio operis faciendi type 1 (carry for
hire); Locatio operis faciendi type 2 (work on goods for hire); Mandatum
(convey without charge); Locatio custodiae (keep for reward/hire of
custody); Constructive bailments (non-consensual)

Depositum Goods delivered to keep WITHOUT FEE --- Khan v Grocutt

Commodatum Lending for borrower\'s use WITHOUT CHARGE --- Coughlin v
Gillinson (machine owner strictly liable to injured workers from the
bailment relationship)

Locatio et conductio HIRING goods to another for reward --- Chapleton v
Barry UDC; Chapman v GWR

Vadium PLEDGING goods (pawnbroker) --- Donald v Suckling (valuable paper
can be pledged)

Locatio operis faciendi --- distinction Type 1: goods bailed to be
CARRIED for hire (transportation). Type 2: goods bailed for something to
be DONE TO THEM for hire (repair, cleaning). Both are locatio operis
faciendi.

Mandatum Goods delivered to be conveyed/for purpose WITHOUT CHARGE ---
Coggs v Bernard itself

Locatio custodiae What would otherwise be a depositum but FOR REWARD ---
hire of custody --- Chapman v GWR

Bailment --- burden of proof REVERSED: bailee must prove they took
REASONABLE CARE --- not for claimant to prove negligence. Travers v
Cooper.

Bailment --- liability for servants Bailee FULLY responsible for acts of
ALL servants --- Port Swettenham Authority v TW Wu. No need to
separately prove vicarious liability conditions.

Bailment --- liability for theft Bailee IS LIABLE if bailed property is
stolen. NO novus actus interveniens defence (unlike ordinary negligence)
--- British Road Services v Arthur V Crutchley Ltd.

Bailment --- liability for independent contractors Bailee IS LIABLE for
negligence of INDEPENDENT CONTRACTORS engaged --- BRS v AVC. Unlike
ordinary negligence where no vicarious liability for ICs.

Bailment --- sub-bailees Each bailee in chain liable for sub-bailee\'s
acts. Any party in chain can be sued. The Pioneer Container: clause in
original contract allowing sub-bailment \'on any terms\' gives
sub-bailee benefit of exclusion clauses --- without such clause,
sub-bailee has no protection.

Bailment --- jus tertii Bailee ESTOPPED from pleading goods belong to a
third party (not the bailor). Cannot deny bailor\'s title --- Biddle v
Bond.

Bailment --- bailor\'s remedies (1) Breach of contract; (2) Negligence
(reversed burden); (3) Statutory conversion (Torts (Interference with
Goods) Act 1977); (4) Trover; (5) Trespass to chattels

=== CARRIAGE OF GOODS BY SEA ===

Bill of lading --- THREE functions (1) DOCUMENT OF TITLE --- must be
presented to obtain delivery; (2) RECEIPT of goods and their apparent
condition; (3) EVIDENCE of contract of carriage

Bill of lading --- to order (negotiable) Transferable by endorsement.
Holder can claim delivery. Negotiable instrument --- HDC rules apply.

Bill of lading --- straight (non-negotiable) Named consignee. Still MUST
BE PRESENTED for delivery --- The Rafaela S \[2005\] UKHL 11; APL v
Voss. Carrier delivering without presentation = liable for conversion.

Sea waybill NOT a document of title. NOT required for delivery.
Non-negotiable. Can be reproduced. Subject to HV Rules ONLY if clause
paramount incorporated.

Bill of lading --- clean vs claused Clean: goods received in good order
and condition. Claused: notes damage or defect. Clean bill = prima facie
evidence goods were in good condition on shipment.

Bill of lading --- SLAC Shipper\'s Load, Stow and Count --- carrier does
not verify contents; SHIPPER responsible for quantity and loading.

Bareboat (demise) charterparty Charter for ship only; charterer supplies
crew. NOT a contract for carriage of goods by sea at all.

HV Rules --- proper name and source Hague Rules as amended by Brussels
Protocol 1968. Incorporated into English law by CARRIAGE OF GOODS BY SEA
ACT 1971.

HV Rules --- when they apply (Article 10) (1) Bill of lading issued in a
CONTRACTING STATE; OR (2) Carriage FROM a port of a contracting state;
OR (3) INCORPORATED by clause paramount into bill of lading

HV Rules --- extension by COGSA 1971 s.1 (1) UK COASTAL TRADE
(internationality not required); (2) SEA WAYBILLS (if clause paramount
incorporated); (3) DECK CARGO (included unless excluded); (4) LIVE
ANIMALS (included)

HV Rules --- Art 3(1) seaworthiness Exercise DUE DILIGENCE BEFORE and AT
COMMENCEMENT of voyage to: (a) make ship SEAWORTHY; (b) properly MAN,
EQUIP and SUPPLY; (c) make cargo spaces FIT and SAFE. NOT absolute duty.
Seaworthiness only required at BEGINNING of voyage. Includes
cargo-worthiness and crew training. NO breach for latent defects not
discoverable by due diligence.

HV Rules --- Art 3(2) care for cargo Properly and carefully: LOAD,
HANDLE, STOW, CARRY, KEEP CARE FOR, DISCHARGE goods. Duty extends
THROUGHOUT the voyage. Shipper must give special instructions for
special care.

HV Rules --- Art 3(3) issue bill of lading On demand of shipper: issue
bill showing quantity, quality, APPARENT condition of goods. Refers to
APPARENT condition only --- Compania Naviera v Churchill \[1906\].
Master\'s signature does NOT bind shipowner for non-existent cargo ---
Grant v Norway (1851).

HV Rules --- Art 4(4) deviation Carrier liable for loss from deviation
UNLESS: authorised by shipper; saving/attempting to save life; beyond
reasonable control; barratrous conduct of master/crew; industrial action
unconnected with carrier. Leduc v Ward: \'liberty to call\' = ports on
GEOGRAPHICAL ROUTE only; unauthorised deviation = loss of ALL Art 4
exceptions.

HV Rules --- Art 4(2) excepted perils (key ones) (1) Nautical fault ---
act/neglect of master in NAVIGATION AND MANAGEMENT of ship (not cargo);
(2) Fire --- unless actual fault of carrier (Maxine Footwear v CGMM
\[1959\]); (3) Perils of the sea; (4) Act of God; war; public enemies;
(5) Act or omission of SHIPPER; (6) Inherent vice/sufficient packing;
(7) Latent defects not discoverable by due diligence; (8) Barratry of
master/crew

HV Rules --- Art 3(8) VOID to attempt to remove the shipper\'s CHOICE to
enumerate packages/units in bill of lading for limitation purposes.

HV Rules --- Art 4(5) limitation of liability 666.67 SDR per
package/unit OR 2 SDR per kilogram of gross weight --- WHICHEVER IS
HIGHER. Exception: if damage caused with INTENT or RECKLESSLY with
knowledge damage would result --- no limitation. El Greco \[2004\]: bill
recording \'1 x 20ft container\' = 1 unit for limitation purposes (must
enumerate to increase limit).

Burden of proof --- The Hellenic Dolphin \[1978\] --- four steps (1)
SHIPPER proves goods in good condition on shipment (clean bill) but
arrived damaged → prima facie Art 3(2) breach; (2) CARRIER proves an Art
4(2) EXCEPTED PERIL applies; (3) SHIPPER rebuts the peril OR pleads Art
3(1) breach (unseaworthiness); (4) CARRIER proves unseaworthiness was
NOT due to lack of due diligence

Shipper\'s obligations --- HV Rules (1) Pay FREIGHT --- carrier has lien
over cargo for unpaid freight. CFR: seller pays. FOB: liability
transfers to lawful holder/consignee. (2) DANGEROUS GOODS: if shipped
without consent/proper marking → carrier may land, destroy, JETTISON or
render innocuous at any time --- The Giannis NK \[1998\] AC 605.

COGSA 1992 --- rights transfer Transfers CONTRACTUAL RIGHTS to lawful
holder of bill of lading --- same rights as original shipper. Rights
VEST when holder: takes DELIVERY; makes a CLAIM; takes steps ENTAILING
LIABILITY.

Grant v Norway (1851) Master cannot bind shipowner by signing bill of
lading for goods NOT put on board --- no liability to third parties for
non-existent cargo.

Leduc v Ward \[1888\] \'Liberty to call\' clause = ports on GEOGRAPHICAL
ROUTE only. Unauthorised deviation = loss of ALL contractual exceptions.
Carrier fully liable for loss.

The Rafaela S \[2005\] UKHL 11 Straight bill of lading IS a document of
title under HV Rules and COGSA 1971. Must be PRESENTED by consignee to
obtain delivery.

APL v Voss Carrier delivering without presentation of straight bill of
lading = liable for CONVERSION. Carrier bears risk of delivering without
production.

Svenska Traktor v Maritime Agencies \[1953\] \'Liberty to carry on
deck\' ≠ statement goods ARE on deck. HV Rules still apply unless bill
SPECIFICALLY STATES goods on deck AND they are so carried.

El Greco v Mediterranean Shipping \[2004\] Bill recording \'1 x 20ft FCL
container said to contain 200,945 pieces\' → 1 UNIT for limitation
purposes. Must enumerate contents in bill to maximise limitation amount.

=== DISPUTE RESOLUTION ===

Arbitration --- definition A process by which parties CONSENSUALLY
submit a dispute to a NON-GOVERNMENTAL DECISION-MAKER, selected by or
for the parties, to render a FINAL AND BINDING DECISION on the merits,
according to NEUTRAL ADJUDICATORY PROCEDURES giving each party an
opportunity to present their case.

Arbitration --- consent requirement Must be in WRITING or evidenced in
writing: s.5 Arbitration Act 1996; Article 2 New York Convention 1958.
Post-dispute agreement = SUBMISSION AGREEMENT.

Arbitration --- separability doctrine Arbitration agreement is SEPARATE
and INDEPENDENT from the main contract. Survives the main contract being
void/rescinded. Allows arbitrators to rule on validity of underlying
contract without losing jurisdiction.

Arbitration --- non-governmental decision maker Government decision
maker = CHOICE OF COURT AGREEMENT, not arbitration. Arbitrators:
independent and impartial; mainly commercial bar; typically 3-person
panel (1 each, presiding 3rd); majority prevails.

Arbitration --- natural justice requirements Nemo iudex in sua causa (no
BIAS/conflict of interest); audi alteram partem (each party has FAIR
OPPORTUNITY to present case)

Arbitration --- appeal on law NO general right of appeal on law ---
s.69(1) Arbitration Act 1996. Parties CAN contract out of this right.
Facts: BINDING --- no appeal.

Arbitration --- removing arbitrator s.24 Arbitration Act 1996 --- remove
arbitrator on grounds of BIAS or conflict of interest. Courts of SEAT of
arbitration have supervisory jurisdiction.

Arbitration --- ad hoc vs institutional Ad hoc: no institution; maximum
procedural flexibility; governed by national law of seat only.
Institutional: LCIA, ICC, SIAC --- institutional rules apply;
institutional fees payable; arbitrators often from institution\'s panel.

Multi-tier dispute resolution More than one method with escalation.
Typical: negotiation → ICC mediation (45-day window) → ICC arbitration.
Must be CLEAR, UNEQUIVOCAL, UNAMBIGUOUS. Must EXHAUST each stage before
escalating.

Arbitration --- construction of clause \'Arising under\': NARROWER ---
performance disputes only. \'Arising out of or in connection with\':
WIDER --- includes rescission, invalidity, pre-contractual matters.
Fiona Trust v Privalov \[2007\] UKHL 40: old distinction ABOLISHED ---
both construed broadly. Rational parties intend ALL disputes from their
relationship to go to same tribunal.

Arbitration --- advantages (1) Neutral forum --- no home court
advantage; (2) New York Convention 1958: 170+ states, pro-enforcement
bias Art 3 --- no comparable convention for court judgments (Hague 2019
very limited, effective UK 2025); (3) Confidentiality --- private
proceedings; (4) Party autonomy --- choose seat, law, arbitrators; (5)
Expert adjudicators from commercial bar; (6) Less emphasis on public
policy/public interest; (7) Procedural flexibility (especially ad hoc)

Arbitration --- disadvantages (1) No COERCIVE POWERS --- cannot compel
third-party witnesses; jurisdiction limited to parties to agreement; (2)
No body of PRECEDENT develops --- confidentiality means awards not
public; (3) Increasingly EXPENSIVE --- institutional fees + arbitrator
fees + counsel; (4) FINALITY --- limited appeal can be disadvantage if
award wrong on law; (5) Gender and ethnic diversity issues in arbitrator
pool

New York Convention 1958 --- Article 5 (refusal grounds) (1) Party
incapacity or agreement invalid; (2) Insufficient notice or unable to
present case; (3) Award beyond scope of submission; (4) Procedure not in
accordance with agreement; (5) Award not yet binding or set aside by
court of seat. ALSO: contrary to public policy of enforcing state.

Fiona Trust v Privalov \[2007\] UKHL 40 Broad construction of
arbitration clauses. Old \'arising under\' vs \'arising out of\'
distinction ABOLISHED. Rational businessmen intend all disputes from
their relationship to go to same tribunal. Separability: if contract
induced by bribery, arbitration clause still valid to determine that
question.

=== METHODS OF PAYMENT ===

Transferability vs negotiability Transferability: recipient acquires
SAME rights as transferor. Negotiability: HIGHER form --- holder in due
course (HDC) acquires BETTER title than transferor; prior defects in
title do not follow the instrument. Exception to nemo dat.

Nemo dat exception --- negotiable instruments Bona fide purchaser for
value without notice (holder in due course, s.29 BoEA 1882) gets BETTER
title than transferor --- free from prior defects.

Cross cheque (s.81 BoEA 1882) TRANSFERABLE but NOT NEGOTIABLE --- cannot
gain better title than transferor had

Payment --- discharge Discharged when payee\'s bank UNCONDITIONALLY
ACCEPTS payment as creditor --- credits payee\'s account. Until that
moment, risk remains with PAYER.

BACS Bankers\' Automated Clearing Service. BULK, LOWER-VALUE transfers
(wages, pensions). 3-DAY clearing cycle. REVOCABLE up to end of Day 2
before 3:30 PM (before payee\'s bank instructed).

CHAPS Clearing House Automated Payment System. HIGH-VALUE,
TIME-SENSITIVE. REAL-TIME / same day. NOT REVOCABLE once sent. Bank of
England settles between bank accounts.

Philipp v Barclays Bank correctly executes customer\'s instructions
(even if payer gave wrong beneficiary details) → NO LIABILITY. Payer
bears commercial risk of providing incorrect details.

Promissory note (s.83 BoEA 1882) PROMISE TO PAY. Drawer promises to pay
payee fixed sum on fixed/determinable date. Requirements: (1) Fixed sum;
(2) Fixed or determinable date; (3) UNCONDITIONAL; (4) In writing;
signed by maker.

Claydon v Bradley \'Paid back BY \[date\]\' rather than \'ON \[date\]\'
= uncertainty = INVALID promissory note. Must be fixed or determinable
date.

Bill of exchange (s.3 BoEA 1882) ORDER TO PAY. THREE PARTIES: Drawer →
Drawee → Payee. Unconditional order in writing, signed by drawer,
requiring drawee to pay fixed sum at fixed/determinable time to payee.

Cheque (s.73 BoEA 1882) Bill of exchange where the drawee MUST BE A
BANK. Same three-party structure.

Bill of exchange --- seven validity requirements (1) Unconditional
order; (2) In writing; (3) Drawer to drawee; (4) SIGNED BY DRAWER (most
critical); (5) Fixed/determinable time; (6) Fixed sum (digits and
words); (7) Payee specified (named or \'bearer\')

Bill of exchange --- acceptance Drawee SIGNS the bill → obligated to
pay. Until acceptance, only DRAWER is liable. If drawee REFUSES
acceptance → payee can IMMEDIATELY pursue drawer.

Holder in due course (s.29 BoEA 1882) --- FIVE requirements (1) Bill
COMPLETE and REGULAR on its face; (2) Taken BEFORE MATURITY; (3) For
VALUE; (4) In GOOD FAITH; (5) WITHOUT NOTICE of any defect in title of
person negotiating it. Gets title FREE from prior defects.

Transfer of bill of exchange --- two methods DELIVERY ALONE: bearer
instruments. ENDORSEMENT (sign on back): order instruments. Once
endorsed, endorser becomes liable if payee not paid.

=== BANKING LAW ===

Definition of a bank --- UDT v Kirkwood \[1966\] THREE characteristics
(1) Accepting DEPOSITS of money from customers; (2) HONOURING cheques
drawn by customers; (3) COLLECTING cheques for customers

Banker-customer relationship --- nature (Foley v Hill \[1848\])
Contractual DEBTOR-CREDITOR --- NOT trust or fiduciary. Money deposited
= BANK\'S PROPERTY. Bank must REPAY ON DEMAND. Limitation period runs
from REFUSED DEMAND (not time of deposit).

Joachimson v Swiss Bank Corporation \[1921\] --- terms of demand
Repayment: (1) ON DEMAND; (2) At the RELEVANT BRANCH; (3) During BANKING
HOURS. Mutual obligations flow from this.

Definition of \'customer\' --- modern approach Person who OPENS A BANK
ACCOUNT. Relationship begins when bank AGREES TO OPEN ACCOUNT --- Woods
v Martins Bank \[1959\].

Definition of \'customer\' --- old approach (pre-modern) Required
HABITUAL DEALINGS --- Taxation of Commissioners v English, Scottish and
Australian Bank \[1920\]. Superseded.

Bank\'s duty to pay on demand Must pay when customer demands VALIDLY ---
failure = breach of contract

Bank\'s duty to follow mandate --- grounds to REFUSE without liability
(1) Insufficient FUNDS; (2) Unclear/AMBIGUOUS instructions (London Joint
Stock Bank v Macmillan \[1918\]); (3) Reasonably suspects FRAUD (Lipkin
Gorman v Karpnale \[1989\]); (4) STALE cheque; (5) Statutory/court
restriction (POCA 2002; bankruptcy; third party debt order; freezing
order)

Cooper v National Westminster Bank \[2009\] Bank must SEEK CLARITY on
ambiguous/unclear instructions --- cannot simply refuse without first
attempting to clarify.

Countermand (s.75 BoEA 1882) Revocation of payment instruction. Must be:
CLEAR; UNAMBIGUOUS; RECEIVED IN TIME. Westminster Bank v Hilton
\[1926\]: effective countermand. Reade v RBS \[1992\]: minor errors do
NOT invalidate. Curtice v London & Midland Bank \[1908\]: fundamental
error MAY invalidate.

Matthews v Brown & Co \[1984\] POSTDATED cheque --- bank paying cheque
before the date written on its face → liable to customer. Postdating =
instruction not to pay before that date.

Collecting instruments --- standard (s.4 Cheques Act 1957) Collecting
bank protected from conversion liability if it acts in GOOD FAITH
WITHOUT NEGLIGENCE. Test: would a REASONABLE BANKER act this way? ---
Marfani v Midland Bank \[1968\].

Duty of confidentiality --- Tournier v National Provincial \[1924\] ---
FOUR exceptions Disclosure permitted when: (1) Required under LAW (POCA
2002, Insolvency Act etc.); (2) Required by duty to the PUBLIC; (3)
Necessary to protect the BANK\'S OWN INTERESTS; (4) Made with
CUSTOMER\'S EXPRESS OR IMPLIED CONSENT

Customer\'s duty re drawing cheques --- London Joint Stock Bank v
Macmillan \[1918\] Draw cheques with REASONABLE CARE to prevent fraud or
alteration. Leaving gap = CUSTOMER bears loss from alteration.

Customer\'s duty re forgery --- Greenwood v Martins Bank Must notify
bank of forgery PROMPTLY. Long delay = ESTOPPED from claiming back funds
--- impliedly consented by inaction.

Bank\'s right of set-off --- Stoney Stanton Supplies v Midland Bank
\[1966\] Bank may combine two accounts of same customer in same CAPACITY
--- apply credit against debit. No CUSTOMER CONSENT required.
Fundamental incident of banker-customer relationship.

Woods v Martins Bank \[1959\] Relationship can extend beyond
debtor-creditor to ADVICE GIVING. Where bank undertakes to advise, owes
DUTY OF CARE. Banking business cannot be exhaustively defined ---
evolves with commercial practice.

Great Western Railway v London & County Banking \[1901\] Collecting bank
may be liable for CONVERSION if customer had no title to cheque being
collected.

Bank regulation --- two regulators PRA (Prudential Regulation
Authority): adequate LIQUIDITY and correct RISK ALLOCATION. FCA
(Financial Conduct Authority): CUSTOMER PROTECTION, market integrity,
competition, fairness.

=== INSOLVENCY ===

Insolvency --- cash flow test (s.123(1)(e) IA 1986) Debtor UNABLE TO PAY
DEBTS AS THEY FALL DUE --- even if assets are sufficient. BNY Corporate
Trustees v Eurosail \[2013\] UKSC 28: assets do not save company that
cannot pay when due. Typically links to ADMINISTRATION (rescue).

Insolvency --- balance sheet test (s.123(2) IA 1986) Liabilities EXCEED
ASSETS including CONTINGENT and PROSPECTIVE obligations. Long-term
financial health. Company may currently pay day-to-day but still fail
this test. Typically links to LIQUIDATION.

Insolvency --- four procedures (1) LIQUIDATION --- terminal; office
holder = liquidator; (2) ADMINISTRATION --- rescue/restructure; office
holder = administrator; (3) CVA --- agreement with creditors; office
holder = supervisor; (4) MORATORIUM (CIGA 2020) --- temporary
protection; office holder = monitor

Liquidation Terminal procedure. Company CEASES TO EXIST. Liquidator
gathers/maximises assets; distributes to creditors in statutory order;
dissolves company.

Administration --- three objectives (Sch B1 Para 3 IA 1986) in order (1)
RESCUE company as going concern; if not reasonably practicable → (2)
BETTER RESULT for creditors than winding up; if not practicable → (3)
REALISE assets for secured/preferential creditors. Re Atlantic Computer
Systems plc: individual enforcement SUSPENDED during administration ---
creditors need court\'s leave.

CVA (Company Voluntary Arrangement) Formal agreement between company and
creditors to compromise debts. Directors REMAIN IN CONTROL. Supervisor
oversees. Avoids liquidation; structured repayment plan.

Moratorium (CIGA 2020) Temporary protection from creditor enforcement.
Monitor oversees. Enables restructuring/refinancing without immediate
creditor pressure.

Priority waterfall --- full order (IA 1986 s.175 & Sch 6) 1st: FIXED
CHARGE holders; 2nd: INSOLVENCY EXPENSES (s.176ZA); 3rd: PREFERENTIAL
CREDITORS (employees: wages/holiday/pensions; HMRC: PAYE and VAT); 4th:
PRESCRIBED PART FUND (s.176A --- carved from floating charge
realisations for unsecured); 5th: FLOATING CHARGE holders; 6th:
UNSECURED CREDITORS (pari passu); 7th: STATUTORY INTEREST (frozen at
insolvency, statutory rate); 8th: SUBORDINATED CREDITORS; 9th:
SHAREHOLDERS

Fixed charge --- key features Over SPECIFIC IDENTIFIED asset(s). Cannot
be disposed of without creditor\'s consent. Must REGISTER within 21 DAYS
--- Companies Act Part 25; if unregistered, may be UNENFORCEABLE at
insolvency. Highest priority after insolvency expenses.

Floating charge --- key features Over CLASS of CHANGING ASSETS (e.g.
stock, debtors). CRYSTALLISES on insolvency or trigger event. Ranks
BELOW fixed charges. Prescribed part (s.176A) ring-fenced for unsecured
creditors from floating charge realisations. Also must register within
21 days.

Pari passu --- rule (s.107 IA 1986) Creditors in the SAME CATEGORY share
assets PROPORTIONATELY to claim size. Not equality of amount ---
equality of TREATMENT. MANDATORY --- cannot be altered by contract.
British Eagle Airlines: contractual term altering distribution hierarchy
among same-class creditors = VOID. Insolvency fairness OVERRIDES party
autonomy.

Subordinated creditors Voluntarily accept LOWER RANKING --- just above
shareholders. PERMITTED (putting self in less favourable position is
allowed --- British Eagle does not apply). Reasons: higher interest rate
in return for risk; encourage another lender to enter.

Anti-deprivation rule Assets cannot be removed from company\'s estate
through contractual arrangements favouring one creditor over the
collective. Vesting clause on INSOLVENCY = VOID. Vesting clause on
DEFAULT OF PAYMENT (not insolvency) = ENFORCEABLE. Belmont Park v BNY
\[2012\] SC: if parties genuinely entered commercial agreement in GOOD
FAITH (not designed to evade insolvency), clause may be upheld ---
substance of transaction matters.

Ayerst v C&K Construction \[1976\] On insolvency, assets held on TRUST
for creditors --- no longer freely disposable. Excluded from estate:
RETENTION OF TITLE goods (Romalpa); trust property; valid security
interests.

Aluminium Industrie Vaassen v Romalpa Aluminium Retention of title
clause: seller retains OWNERSHIP in goods until paid in full. ROT goods
EXCLUDED from insolvency estate --- seller can recover them.

Directors\' duties on insolvency --- West Mercia Safetywear v Dodd
Directors must prioritise CREDITORS\' INTERESTS (not shareholders) when
insolvency is IMMINENT --- breach = personal liability.

Directors\' duties --- BTI 2014 v Sequana SA Duty to creditors arises
when there is a REAL RISK of insolvency --- NOT only on actual
insolvency.

Wrongful trading (s.214 IA 1986) Director liable if knew or OUGHT TO
HAVE KNOWN company couldn\'t avoid insolvent liquidation and continued
to trade without taking every step to minimise creditor loss. Result:
personal CONTRIBUTION to assets.

Fraudulent trading (s.213 IA 1986) Trading with INTENT TO DEFRAUD
creditors. Criminal sanction + personal liability. Wider third-party
liability than wrongful trading.

s.127 IA 1986 --- void dispositions Disposition of company property
AFTER winding up petition filed = VOID. Grays Inn Construction; Oxford
Pharmaceuticals: assets brought back into estate.

s.238 IA 1986 --- transactions at undervalue Within 2 YEARS (connected
persons) or 6 MONTHS (others) before insolvency. Can be set aside UNLESS
done in good faith to save company (genuine commercial purpose = valid).

s.239 IA 1986 --- preferences Paying one creditor over others when
company insolvent. Voidable. Within 6 MONTHS (unconnected) or 2 YEARS
(connected persons).

BNY Corporate Trustees v Eurosail \[2013\] UKSC 28 Cash flow insolvency
test --- assets do NOT save company that cannot pay debts when due.
Inability to pay when due = insolvent under s.123(1)(e).

British Eagle Airlines (pari passu) Parties cannot contract to give one
creditor better distribution than insolvency law provides. Insolvency
fairness overrides party autonomy. Clearing house arrangement void ---
altered the pari passu distribution.

Belmont Park Investments v BNY Corporate Trustee \[2012\] SC
Anti-deprivation rule --- softened strict approach. Genuine commercial
agreement in good faith (not designed to evade insolvency) may be upheld
even if removes assets on insolvency. Substance of transaction over
form.

Re Atlantic Computer Systems plc Administration = suspension of
individual enforcement. Creditors (e.g. landlords) require COURT\'S
LEAVE to enforce remedies during administration.
