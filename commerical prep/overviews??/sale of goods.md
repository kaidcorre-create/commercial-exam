### cases

#### Clegg v. Olle Andersson (t/a Nordic Marine) [2003] EWCA Civ 320
- Cleggs bought a new Malo 42 yacht for £236,000, specified to have a shoal draught keel with 5.5t ballast per manufacturer's standard specification
- On delivery (August 2000), the keel was found to be 607kg overweight
- Both experts agreed the overweight keel had an adverse effect on rig safety and that increased rig loads were unacceptable to the rig manufacturers (Selden Masts)
- Remedial options were not communicated to the Cleggs until 14 February 2001; they rejected the yacht on 6 March 2001 via solicitors' letter
- The trial judge found no breach of condition and held the right to reject had been lost; the Court of Appeal reversed both findings
-  Satisfactory quality (s.14(2) Sale of Goods Act 1979):
	- A yacht requiring more than minimal remedial work due to an overweight keel adversely affecting rig safety did not meet the standard a reasonable person would regard as satisfactory; even if the cost of repair was relatively low (c.£1,680). Low repair cost is not a reliable indicator of satisfactory quality.
- Right to reject and acceptance (s.35 SGA 1979): 
	- The 1994 amendments to s.35 strengthened buyers' rights to reject. Time for acceptance does not run while the buyer is still awaiting information promised by the seller necessary to make an informed decision. Asking for or agreeing to repair does not constitute acceptance (s.35(6)(a)).
- Intimation of acceptance 
	- Sailing the yacht and telling the seller you "like it" does not amount to acceptance where the buyer was still clearly seeking information about a known defect. 
	- Insuring goods (especially where required by a loan agreement) and attempting to register them are not necessarily acts inconsistent with the seller's reversionary interest.

#### Underwood Ltd v. Burgh Castle Brick & Cement Syndicate [1922] 1 KB 343
- Facts
	- Sellers agreed to sell a 30-tonne horizontal condensing engine, fixed by bolts and embedded in a concrete floor, to be delivered free on rail (f.o.r.) in London
	- Before it could be moved it had to be unfastened (2 days' work) and then dismantled (a further 2 weeks' work and ~£100 expense)
	- While being loaded onto a railway truck, the bedplate was accidentally broken; buyers refused to accept it
	- Sellers sued for the price, arguing property had passed on contract
- Legal Principles
	- Deliverable state (s.18, r.1 Sale of Goods Act 1893):
		- Property in specific goods passes at contract only if the goods are in a "deliverable state." 
		- A "deliverable state" does not simply mean the goods are complete in themselves
		- It means they are in a state in which the buyer would be bound to take delivery under the contract terms. 
		- An engine embedded in concrete requiring significant work and expense to detach and dismantle is not in a deliverable state.
	- s.18, r.2:
		- Where the seller must do something to put goods into a deliverable state, property does not pass until that is done and the buyer has notice.
		- The sellers therefore bore the risk of accidental damage during loading.
	- Parties' intention:
		- Even if r.1 had applied, the circumstances (cost, risk, and nature of the work) showed an intention that property would not pass until the engine was safely placed on rail
		- s.18 rules are default rules, displaced by contrary intention.

#### Philip Head & Sons Ltd v. Showfronts Ltd [1970] 1 Lloyd’s Rep 140
- Facts
	- Defendant building contractors bought carpet from the plaintiffs to be laid at their showrooms
	- Carpet was delivered to the showrooms, then collected by the plaintiffs' sub-contractor for stitching, returned in heavy bales, and subsequently stolen
	- Plaintiffs sued for the price, arguing property had passed to the defendants
- Legal Principles
	- Deliverable state (s.18, r.5(1) Sale of Goods Act 1893):
		- Carpet returned in heavy bales and not yet laid (or ready to be laid) was not in a "deliverable state" 
		- It still required further work (laying) before the buyer would be obliged to take delivery of it in that form.
		- Property therefore had not passed to the buyers.
	- The risk of theft accordingly remained with the seller (plaintiffs), and the defendants were not liable for the price.
	- Reinforces the principle from _Underwood_ that "deliverable state" is assessed by reference to the contractual obligation to accept, not merely by the physical existence of the goods.

#### Healy v. Howlett & Sons [1917] 1 KB 337
- Facts
	- Irish fish exporter (Healy) sold 20 boxes of hard, bright mackerel to Howlett & Sons (Billingsgate salesmen)
	- Healy consigned 190 boxes by rail from Valentia to his own order at Holyhead, with telegraphed instructions to the railway company there to distribute 20 boxes to the defendants and the rest to two other buyers
	- Train was delayed; fish missed the boat to Holyhead and arrived in London in an unmerchantable condition
	- After the delay, railway officials at Holyhead earmarked 20 boxes for the defendants — but by then the fish were already deteriorating
	- An invoice bearing the words "at sole risk of purchaser after putting fish on rail here" was sent after the contract was concluded
- Legal Principles
	- Appropriation of unascertained goods (s.18, r.5 Sale of Goods Act 1893):
		- Property in unascertained goods passes only upon unconditional appropriation of specific goods to the contract with the assent of the other party. 
		- Where 190 boxes were consigned for three separate buyers with no identification of which 20 belonged to the defendants at the point of loading, there was no appropriation at Valentia and so property did not pass there.
	- Post-damage appropriation is ineffective
		- The earmarking done by the railway company at Holyhead occurred after the deterioration had already begun.
		- An appropriation made after damage cannot pass property or shift risk retrospectively to the buyer.
	- Invoices sent after contract formation:
		- Words added to an invoice after the contract is concluded (here, placing risk on the buyer from the point of rail) do not form part of the contract and cannot bind the buyer.
	- Risk follows property since property had not passed, the loss fell on the seller.

#### Carlos Federspiel & Co SA v. Charles Twigg & Co Ltd [1957] 1 Lloyd’s Rep 240
- Facts
	- Plaintiffs (Costa Rican buyers) ordered goods (bicycles) from the English seller and paid in advance; contract was FOB with some CIF features, goods to be shipped to Costa Rica
	- Seller packed the goods, marked crates with the buyers' name, registered them for consignment, and booked shipping space on a named vessel
	- Before the goods reached the port, a receiver was appointed by the seller's debenture holders and refused to deliver
	- Buyers argued property had already passed to them (by packing/marking); seller's receiver argued it had not
- Legal Principles
	- Appropriation and passing of property on FOB contracts:
		- Under an FOB contract, the parties' intention is generally that property passes on shipment (loading aboard the vessel), not on prior packing or marking. 
		- Preparatory acts (packing, labelling, booking freight) are not sufficient unconditional appropriation to pass property under s.18, r.5 Sale of Goods Act 1893.
	- Appropriation must be "unconditional":
		- The seller retains the ability to substitute or redirect goods until the point of actual shipment on an FOB contract; prior steps do not irrevocably appropriate goods to the contract.
	- Parties' intention is paramount:
		- Courts look to the overall contractual intention (particularly the FOB/shipment term) to determine when property was meant to pass, and preparatory acts will not override that intention.
		- Important for insolvency scenarios: 
			- where a seller becomes insolvent before shipment, buyers who have paid in advance will generally not have acquired title and will rank only as unsecured creditors.

#### Hartley v. Hymans [1920] 3 KB 475
- Facts
	- Written contract (July 1918) for Hartley (seller) to supply Hymans (buyer) with 11,000 lb of cotton yarn, delivery to begin September 1918 at 1,100 lb/week, completing by 15 November 1918
	- Contract expressly stated failure to deliver within stipulated time rendered it liable to cancellation
	- Seller delivered virtually nothing on time — first delivery was 26 October 1918 (550 lb), and continued making late partial deliveries into February 1919
	- Throughout this period the buyer complained by letter but continued to urge further deliveries and treat the contract as alive, incurring further expense in reliance on it
	- On 13 March 1919, with no prior reasonable notice, buyer cancelled the contract abruptly
	- Seller sued for damages for wrongful cancellation; damages agreed at £563
- Legal Principles
	- Time of the essence in commercial contracts:
		- In ordinary commercial contracts for the sale of goods, time of delivery is prima facie of the essence. 
		- A seller who fails to deliver on time loses the right to insist on the buyer accepting late delivery.
	- Waiver of time stipulation:
		- A buyer who, after the contractual deadline has passed, continues to demand deliveries, accepts them, and writes letters treating the contract as alive thereby waives the right to insist on the original delivery period. 
		- Where the contract falls within s.4 Sale of Goods Act 1893 (requiring writing), the waiver must be evidenced in writing and here the defendant's own letters provided that written evidence.
	- Estoppel:
		- Where a party by their letters and conduct leads the other to believe the contract is still subsisting and the other acts to their detriment in reliance on that belief, the first party is estopped from asserting the original time limit.
	- Implied new agreement:
		- Where the original time has been waived, a new agreement is implied that delivery may take place within a reasonable time
		- the buyer must give notice fixing that reasonable time before cancelling. 
		- The buyer here gave no such notice, making the abrupt cancellation of 13 March 1919 wrongful.
	- Notice to terminate:
		- Once a party has extended time (by waiver, estoppel or implied agreement), they cannot simply cancel without giving the other party reasonable notice and a reasonable period in which to perform.

#### Behrend & Co Ltd v. Produce Brokers Co Ltd [1920] 3 KB 530
- Facts
	- Sellers sold two parcels of Egyptian cotton seed (176 tons and 400 tons) to buyers under two contracts, to be shipped from Alexandria and delivered in London to buyers' craft alongside
	- Ship (Port Inglis) arrived in London on 13 October 1919; buyers paid and presented bills of lading; ship began discharging on 14 October
	- On 17 and 18 October only 15 tons and 22 tons were delivered (small fractions of the total); the ship then sailed to Hull with the remainder of both parcels still on board to discharge other Hull-bound cargo
	- Ship returned to London about 14 days later and tendered the balance, but buyers refused it
	- Buyers retained what had been delivered and claimed repayment of the price paid for the undelivered balance
	- Umpire in arbitration found for buyers; question was whether they were bound to accept the balance on the ship's return
- Legal Principles
	- **Buyer's right to delivery of entire indivisible parcel from ship:** 
		- Where goods are sold for delivery from a vessel alongside, once the ship arrives and begins delivery, the buyer is entitled to receive the whole of an indivisible parcel before the ship leaves the port. Normal incidental delays (stowage, turn-taking) must be tolerated, but the seller cannot unilaterally interrupt delivery and sail away without breaching the contract.
	- **Effect of partial delivery and right to reject balance (s.30 Sale of Goods Act 1893):** 
		- Where a seller delivers a short quantity, the buyer may either reject all (and recover the full price) or keep what has been delivered and reject the balance (paying only for what is kept and recovering the price of the undelivered portion). The seller's argument that the buyer must return the delivered portion before rejecting the rest was rejected.
	- **Departure of ship constitutes failure to deliver:**
		- The sailing of the Port Inglis to Hull with the bulk of the goods still on board was a failure to deliver, not merely a delay — notwithstanding the promise to return. The contract was for delivery in London upon arrival, and that obligation could not be deferred by the seller's own commercial arrangements with the shipowner.
	- **Buyers need not accept late tender:** 
		- Having validly rejected, the buyers were not bound to accept the balance when the ship returned two weeks later.

#### Varley v. Whipp [1900] 1 QB 513
- Facts
	- Plaintiff offered to sell defendant a second-hand self-binder reaping machine for £21; the defendant had never seen it
	- Plaintiff described it as having been new the previous year and used to cut only 50-60 acres
	- The machine was at Upton at the time; the plaintiff did not yet own it and bought it immediately after the deal
	- On delivery, defendant wrote complaining it was a very old machine that had been mended and did not match the description; after some correspondence he returned it
	- Plaintiff sued for the price; county court judge held the misdescription was only a breach of warranty (not a ground for rejection) and gave judgment for the plaintiff
- Legal Principles
	- Sale by description (s.13 Sale of Goods Act 1893): 
		- The court held that "sale by description" applies to all cases where the buyer has not seen the goods but relies solely on the seller's description not just sales of unascertained goods. 
		- The section therefore applied here even though the machine was a specific, identified item.
	- Description vs collateral warranty:
		- The key distinction is between statements that form part of the description (which trigger the s.13 implied condition) and mere collateral warranties (which give a remedy in damages but not rejection). 
		- Where a buyer purchases something they have never seen, the descriptive statements used to identify the goods form part of the description, not collateral warranties. 
		- Channell J used the horse example: saying a horse is "four years old" is descriptive; saying it is "sound" is a collateral warranty.
		- Implied condition, not warranty: A breach of s.13 is a breach of condition, not merely warranty
		- giving the buyer the right to reject the goods entirely rather than only sue for damages.
	- Property does not pass where implied condition is breached:
		- The seller cannot make property pass by putting goods on a railway that do not fulfil the implied condition. At the earliest, property could pass only on acceptance by the buyer — and the buyer here never accepted the machine.
	- No acceptance: 
		- The buyer's letter of 2 July complaining about the machine did not amount to acceptance (and probably not even rejection at that point). Since he never accepted, property never passed and the seller could not recover the price.
#### Grant v. Australian Knitting Mills Ltd [1936] AC 85
- Facts
	- Dr Grant, a medical practitioner in Adelaide, bought woollen underwear ("Golden Fleece" brand) from John Martin & Co (retailers) manufactured by Australian Knitting Mills
	- He began wearing the underwear on 28 June 1931 and developed severe dermatitis the next day, which progressively worsened over many months — he was bedridden for 17 weeks and at times feared to be at risk of death
	- The dermatitis was caused by excess free sulphites negligently left in the garments during the manufacturing process (a neutralisation step had failed or been poorly applied)
	- The defect was latent and undetectable by any reasonable examination
	- Grant sued both the retailers (in contract) and the manufacturers (in tort)
	- The Supreme Court of South Australia found for Grant; the High Court of Australia reversed; the Privy Council restored the original judgment
- Legal Principles
	- Implied conditions under s.14 Sale of Goods Act (fitness for purpose and merchantable quality):
		- The retailers were liable in contract under both exceptions to s.14. Under exception (i) (fitness for purpose), reliance on a retailer's skill and judgment is inferred from the act of going to a shop 
		- the buyer need not state the purpose expressly where there is only one ordinary purpose for the goods (here, being worn next the skin). 
		- Under exception (ii) (merchantable quality), goods are not merchantable merely because they appear satisfactory
			- they must actually be fit for their only proper use.
			- A latent defect that cannot be detected on ordinary examination does not prevent the implied condition from applying; the merchantable quality condition covers defects not reasonably discoverable on examination.
	- Sale by description (s.14(ii)):
		- The Privy Council confirmed (extending _Varley v Whipp_) that a sale by description can occur even when goods are displayed before the buyer on a counter
		- the goods are sold by description so long as they are sold not merely as specific things but as things corresponding to a description (e.g. woollen undergarments).
	- **Defect easily cured is still a breach:** 
		- A defect that could be remedied (e.g. by washing) is as serious as one that cannot
		- the statute requires the goods to be merchantable in the state in which they were sold and delivered.
#### Harlingdon & Leinster Enterprises Ltd v. Christoper Hull Fine Art Ltd [1991] 1 QB 564
- Facts
	- Christopher Hull, an art dealer with no expertise in German expressionist art, was asked to sell two paintings attributed to Gabriele Münter. 
	- He contacted Harlingdon & Leinster, known specialists in German expressionist art, and described the paintings as being by Münter.
	- Hull made clear to the buyers' representative, Runkel, that he knew nothing about Münter and was not an expert.
	- Runkel inspected the paintings and agreed to buy one for £6,000, relying on his own judgment. 
	- The painting later proved to be a forgery, worth only £50–£100. The buyers sought to recover the purchase price.
- Legal Issues and Principles
	- Sale by Description - s.13(1) Sale of Goods Act 1979
		- was the contract  a "sale by description" within s.13(1), which implies a condition that goods correspond with their description. The majority (Nourse and Slade LJJ) held it was not.
			- The word "by" in "sale by description" is significant
				- the description must have sufficient influence on the sale to become an essential term of the contract, not merely a label or passing reference
			- The buyer's reliance on the description is the "natural index" of a sale by description, though the test is objective, asking whether it was within the reasonable contemplation of the parties that the buyer was relying on the description
			- Where a buyer exercises independent judgment and effectively buys the goods "as they are," there is no sale by description even if a description was made during negotiations
			- The fact that both parties are dealers in the same class of goods is a relevant factor weighing against treating an attribution as a contractual term
			- Stuart-Smith LJ dissented powerfully, arguing that once a description is given, only clear words can withdraw it as a contractual term, and Hull's disclaimer of expertise was insufficient to negate the earlier attribution
	- Merchantable Quality — s.14(2) and (6) Sale of Goods Act 1979
		- Section 14(2) implied a condition of merchantable quality. Section 14(6) defined this as fitness for the purpose or purposes for which goods of that kind are commonly bought, having regard to description, price, and other relevant circumstances.
			- Nourse LJ held the painting was not unmerchantable: it could still be resold (for £50–£100) and appreciated aesthetically, and the buyers could not use their own erroneous judgment as to value to render it unfit
			- Slade LJ took a more direct approach: since the only complaint related to the identity of the artist and that was not a contractual term, s.14 had no application at all — the buyers could not enter by the "back door of s.14" having failed at the "front door of s.13"
			- Stuart-Smith LJ dissented, finding that both parties understood the purpose of the sale was resale by dealers at £6,000 as a Münter, and it was not a reasonable expectation that a virtually worthless fake was fit for that purpose
