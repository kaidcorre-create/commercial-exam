### cases

#### **Owen v Tate [1976] QB 402**
- D borrowed £350 from a bank
- P volunteered to pay the £350 to the bank without D's knowledge
- Bank applied P's payment to D's debt
- could P recover that amount from D afterwards?
- volunteering to pay (on behalf of another) will not establish a right of reimbursement

#### **TSB Bank of Scotland plc v Welwyn Hatfield DC [1993] 2 Bank LR 267 (QB)**

#### **Miller v Race [1758]** 
- bank notes were large and not trusted as much as were new
- bank note was posted and then stolen
- bank of england were notified that it was stolen, if it were presented they shouldn't honour it
- miller (inn manager - inns acted as exchanges) accepted it and gave coins
- presented it to bank of england who refused to honour it
- court find that were a party takes payment instrument in good faith, provides consideration for it, has no notice of defect, then they become a valid owner of it (defect free)

#### **Tayeb v HSBC [2004] EWHC 1529 (Comm)**
- tayeb sold an asset and was owed 1million
- the buyer wanted to pay this via CHAPS]
- tayebs bank recieved notifiaction to credit his account with 1 million and the "logical acknowledgement message" was automatically transmitted to the payers bank
- because of the size of the trasnfer, tayebs accoutn was not automatically credited
- hsbc became suspicious of thet money and placed a stop on the account
- they debited the account and returned the funds
- the buyer was recredited with the money and would not repay
- tayeb sued hsbc claiming that by unconditionally crediting the account, hsbc had acknowledged him as its creditor
- the bank was liable
- The CHAPS rules allow for the return of a transfer where the transfer cannot be applied to the payee’s account or where the transfer has been made in error; the rules do not grant a right, nor is one to be implied, which gives the receiving bank a discretion to reverse a transfer on suspicions of money laundering

#### **Philipp v Barclays [2023] UKSC 25**
- phillips instructs for 30k
- instructions were wrong
- court found that risk is borne by sender unless bank has made a mistake

#### **Tidal Energy Ltd v. Bank of Scotland Plc [2014] EWCA Civ 1107**
- tidal wanted to pay supplier more than 210,000
- instructed bank to transfer by chaps and gave a chaps form giving the name, bank name, bank sort code and account number
- these numbers were given by somebody purporting to be the supplier but who was a fraudster
- the bank trasnferred using the suppiled numbvers
- tidal sued and said that the bankl was liable as the numbers didnt match the name and so the money should not have been sent
- the court held that the terms if the agency between customer and bank were that the bank would carry out the customers instructions on the basis of the chaps rules and standard banking practicse.
- the bank has acted within its mandate
