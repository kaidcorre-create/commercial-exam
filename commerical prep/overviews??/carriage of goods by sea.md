### cases
#### Grant v Norway (1851) 10 CB 665
- **Facts**:
    - The master of the ship _Belle_ signed a bill of lading for 12 bales of silk that had never actually been put on board the vessel.
    - The plaintiffs, relying on the truth of the bill of lading, made financial advances to the holders of the bill (Biale, Koch, & Co.).
    - When the ship arrived and failed to deliver the silk, the plaintiffs sued the shipowners for the lost value.
- **Law/Legal Principles**:
    - The master of a ship has no authority—apparent or otherwise—to sign a bill of lading for goods that have not been shipped.
    - Because the master’s authority is limited to signing for goods actually received, the shipowner is not liable to third parties (even bona fide indorsees for value) who suffer loss by relying on a bill of lading signed for non-existent cargo.
#### Compania Naviera Vascongada v Churchill [1906] 1 KB 237
- **Facts**:
    - A cargo of timber was shipped in a badly stained condition due to petroleum.
    - Despite this visible damage, the master signed a bill of lading stating the goods were "shipped in good order and condition".
    - The defendants took the bills of lading as indorsees and paid for the goods, later discovering the damage.
- **Law/Legal Principles**:
    - A statement in a bill of lading that goods are "shipped in good order and condition" refers to their _apparent_ external condition.
    - The shipowner is **estopped** from later denying the truth of that statement against an indorsee who has taken the bill for value.
    - The master has the authority to bind the shipowner to such representations of apparent condition.
#### Leduc v Ward [1888] 20 QBD 475
- **Facts**:
    - Goods were shipped from Fiume to Dunkirk under a bill of lading giving the ship "liberty to call at any ports in any order".
    - Instead of sailing directly to Dunkirk, the ship deviated to Glasgow and was lost in a storm off the coast of Scotland.
    - The cargo owners sued for the loss, while the shipowners argued the deviation was permitted by the clause.
- **Law/Legal Principles**:
    - The bill of lading is the primary evidence of the contract of carriage, and its terms cannot be varied by extrinsic evidence of prior oral agreements.
    - A "liberty to call" clause is generally interpreted as referring to ports that are substantially on the geographical route of the named voyage.
    - Unauthorized deviation deprives the carrier of the protection of contractual exceptions (like "perils of the sea"), making them liable for the loss of the cargo.
#### The Rafaela S [2005] UKHL 11
- **Facts**:
    - Machinery was shipped from South Africa to the USA under a "straight" bill of lading, which named a specific consignee and was marked non-negotiable.
    - The cargo was damaged during the voyage.
    - The carrier argued that because it was a "straight" bill, it was not a "bill of lading" under the Carriage of Goods by Sea Act 1971, meaning the Hague-Visby liability limits did not apply.
- **Law/Legal Principles**:
    - A straight bill of lading is a "bill of lading or any similar document of title" for the purposes of the Hague and Hague-Visby Rules.
    - Unlike a sea waybill, a straight bill of lading must be presented to the carrier by the consignee to obtain delivery of the goods.
    - The Rules apply to straight bills of lading, ensuring the same level of protection for consignees as negotiable bills.

#### APL v Voss
- **Facts**:
    - A seller (Voss) shipped a car to a buyer in Korea using a straight bill of lading.
    - The carrier (APL) delivered the car to the named buyer without requiring the production of the original bill of lading.
    - The buyer failed to pay the seller, who then sued the carrier for conversion.
- **Law/Legal Principles**
    - Even a non-negotiable "straight" bill of lading remains a document of title that must be presented to the carrier to trigger delivery.
    - A carrier who delivers goods without the production of a straight bill of lading does so at their own risk and is liable to the holder of the bill for the value of the goods.
    - The requirement for production is a key distinction between a straight bill of lading and a sea waybill.
#### Svenska Traktor v Maritime Agencies (Southampton) Ltd [1953] 2 QB 295
- **Facts**:
    - Tractors were shipped from Southampton to Stockholm, with some carried on deck.
    - One tractor was lost overboard during the voyage.
    - The bill of lading contained a general clause stating the ship had "liberty to carry goods on deck" and that the owner would not be responsible for any resulting loss.
- **Law/Legal Principles**:
    - To exclude cargo from the definition of "goods" under the Hague Rules (Art I(c)), the bill of lading must specifically **state** that the goods are being carried on deck and they must be **so carried**.
    - A mere "liberty to carry on deck" clause is not a statement that the goods _are_ being carried on deck; therefore, the Hague Rules still apply to protect the cargo owner.
    - The carrier remains liable for loss if they fail to properly and carefully carry and care for the goods as required by Article III, r. 2.

#### Actis Co Ltd v Sanko Steamship Co Ltd (The Aquacharm) [1982] 1 WLR 119
- **Facts**:
    - The vessel _Aquacharm_ was ordered to load coal to the maximum draught allowed for the Panama Canal.
    - The master overloaded the ship, causing it to exceed the canal's permitted draught.
    - The ship was refused entry and had to wait while part of the cargo was transhipped to another vessel to lighten the load.
- **Law/Legal Principles**:
    - "Seaworthiness" (Hague Rules Art III, r. 1) relates to the physical state and fitness of the vessel itself, not to the specific way it is loaded for a particular segment of the voyage (provided the loading does not make the ship unsafe).
    - The delay caused by the overloading was a "neglect or default of the master... in the management of the ship," for which the owners were exempted from liability under Art IV, r. 2(a).

#### Maxine Footwear Co. v Canadian Government Merchant Marine [1959] AC 589
#### The Xantho (1887) 12 App. Cas. 503
- **Facts**:
    - The ship _Xantho_ foundered after a collision with another vessel, resulting in the total loss of the cargo.
    - The cargo owners sued for non-delivery.
    - The shipowners argued the loss fell under the bill of lading exception for "perils of the sea".
- **Law/Legal Principles**:
    - The term "perils of the sea" has the same meaning in a bill of lading as it does in a marine insurance policy.
    - A collision with another vessel is a "peril of the sea," even if it is not caused by extraordinary weather.
    - The shipowner is excused if the collision occurred without their fault; however, if the carrying ship was negligent, the exception cannot be relied upon.
#### The Giannis NK [1998] AC 605
- **Facts**:
    - Shippers loaded a cargo of groundnuts that was, unknown to them, infested with Khapra beetle.
    - The infestation did not physically damage other cargo but caused the vessel to be quarantined and the entire cargo to be dumped at sea by order of the authorities.
    - The carrier sued the shippers for the resulting delay and expenses.
- **Law/Legal Principles**:
    - Under the Hague Rules (Art IV, r. 6), a shipper is **strictly liable** for shipping "dangerous goods" even if they are unaware of the danger.
    - The definition of "goods of a dangerous nature" is not limited to items that cause physical damage; it includes goods that cause the vessel to be delayed, quarantined, or rendered unusable due to legal or administrative obstacles (such as pest infestation).

#### Scruttons Ltd v Midland Silicones Ltd [1962] AC 446
- **Facts**:
    - Stevedores (Scruttons Ltd) negligently dropped and damaged a drum of chemicals while unloading it from a ship.
    - The bill of lading between the shipper and the shipowners (United States Lines) contained a clause limiting the carrier's liability to $500 per package.
    - The consignees (Midland Silicones Ltd) sued the stevedores in tort for the full value of the damaged goods.
    - The stevedores attempted to rely on the $500 limitation clause found in the bill of lading to which they were not a signatory.
- **Law/Legal Principles**:
    - Under the principle of privity of contract, a person who is not a party to a contract cannot rely on its terms for protection or benefit.
    - Because the stevedores were not parties to the bill of lading contract, they could not benefit from the limitation of liability clause.
    - This ruling underscored the necessity of specific "Himalaya Clauses" to extend contractual protections to third parties like stevedores.
#### The Eurymedon [1975] AC 154
- **Facts**:
    - A drilling machine was damaged by stevedores during unloading; the bill of lading contained a "Himalaya Clause" intended to protect third parties.
    - The consignee sued the stevedores more than a year after the incident, attempting to avoid the one-year time limit for bringing suit under the Hague Rules.
    - The stevedores claimed they were entitled to the same time-bar protection as the carrier.
- **Law/Legal Principles**:
    - A "Himalaya Clause" is legally effective to protect third parties (like stevedores or independent contractors) if four conditions are met:
        1. The bill of lading must clearly state that the third party is intended to be protected.
        2. The carrier must act as an agent for the third party when contracting for these protections.
        3. The carrier must have authority from the third party to act as their agent.
        4. There must be consideration passing from the third party (e.g., the performance of the unloading services).
    - Meeting these criteria allows the third party to rely on contractual immunities, such as the one-year limitation for bringing a claim
#### Marbig Rexel Pty Ltd v ABC Container Line NV (The TNT Express) [1992] 2 Lloyd’s Rep 636
- **Facts**:
    - A shipper-packed container was found to have water-damaged contents upon arrival in Sydney.
    - The damage was caused by defective door seals and a small hole in the floor of the container.
    - The carrier relied on a clause (Clause 10) excluding liability for shipper-packed containers if the defect would have been "apparent on reasonable inspection" by the shipper.
- **Law/Legal Principles**:
    - A "reasonable inspection" for the purposes of such an exclusion clause is one performed by an unskilled person, not an expert surveyor.
    - Defects like degraded door seals or small floor holes are often not apparent to a shipper making a standard check, meaning the carrier cannot rely on the exclusion clause.
    - The carrier remains liable for providing a container that is not fit for the intended carriage if the defects were not obvious to the merchant at the time of packing
#### Great China Metal Industries Co Ltd v Malaysian International Shipping Corp Berhad (1998) 196 CLR 161
- **Facts**:
    - Aluminium coils were damaged during a voyage across the Great Australian Bight due to heavy weather conditions.
    - The carrier relied on the "perils of the sea" exception under the Hague Rules to avoid liability for the damage.
    - The cargo owners argued the carrier was negligent in its stowage planning and should have better anticipated the weather.
- **Law/Legal Principles**:
    - The "perils of the sea" immunity (Art IV r 2(c)) applies to damage caused by the sea that occurs despite the carrier exercising due care.
    - A "peril of the sea" does not have to be an extraordinary or freak occurrence; it can include foreseeable heavy weather if the carrier can prove the ship was seaworthy and the cargo was properly stowed.
    - The core issue is whether the carrier fulfilled its duties under Article III r 2 to "properly and carefully load, handle, stow, carry, keep, care for and discharge" the goods
#### Chubu Asahi Cotton Spinning Co Ltd v The Ship Tenos (1968) 12 FLR 291
#### Minnesota Mining & Manufacturing (Australia) Pty Ltd v The Ship Novoaltaisk [1972] 2 NSMLR 476.
#### El Greco (Australia) Pty Ltd v Mediterranean Shipping Co SA [2004] FCAFC 202
- **Facts**
    - A shipment consisting of approximately 200,000 posters and prints was packed into a single container for a voyage from Australia to Greece.
    - The bill of lading described the container as being "said to contain 200,945 pieces," but only listed "1" in the column specifically designated for the number of packages.
    - The cargo was severely damaged during transit, and the parties disputed how the limitation of liability should be calculated.
- **Law/Legal Principles**:
    - Under Article IV r 5(c) of the amended Hague-Visby Rules, the limit of liability is based on the number of "packages or units" enumerated in the bill of lading as being packed in the container.
    - To effectively enumerate units inside a container for limitation purposes, the bill of lading must clearly identify those specific items as shipping units.
    - If the bill only identifies the container itself as the package (despite stating it contains a certain number of pieces), the container may be treated as the single unit for liability limitation.
#### Brandt v Liverpool [1924] 1 KB 575
- **Facts**:
    - Zinc ashes were shipped under a bill of lading stating they were in "apparent good order and condition".
    - In reality, some bags had been wetted by rain before shipment, leading to heating and necessitating their discharge and reconditioning.
    - The bill of lading was indorsed to a pledgee (Brandt) who made a financial advance based on the document's representations.
    - Brandt took delivery of the goods at the destination, paid the freight and reconditioning charges, and then sued the carrier for damages caused by the initial wetting and subsequent delay.
- **Law/Legal Principles**:
    - The "Brandt v Liverpool" doctrine establishes that a contract can be implied between a carrier and the holder of a bill of lading when that holder takes delivery of the goods and pays the freight or other charges.
    - This implied contract is generally governed by the terms and conditions set out in the bill of lading.
    - The carrier is liable to the indorsee for damages if the goods were not in the condition represented on the bill (e.g., "apparent good order"), as the indorsee relied on that statement to their detriment.
