### cases

#### Fiona Trust & Holding Corp v Privalov [2007] UKHL 40
- ship owner said that he had validly rescinded charterparties entered into with the respondent
- ship owner purported to rescind the charters on the basis that they believed them to have been made through bribery 
- the charters contained a jurisdiction clause in realtion to any dispute arising under the charter
- arbitration clause provided that any party could elect to have any such dispute refer to arbitration
- respondent argued that the issue of whether the claimant was entitled to rescind should be determined by arbitration rather than by a court
- ship owner maintained that it was allowed to bring court proceedings because the question of whether the charters were procured through bribery was nit a dispute arising under the charters, so the arbitration clause did not apply
- if the contract was invalid having been procured through bribery then the jurisdiction clause was not binding
- court "drew a line" under the authorities on the distinction between the clause "disputes arising under" and "disputes arising out of"
- The construction of an arbitration clause had to start from the assumption that the parties, as rational businessmen, were likely to have intended any dispute arising out of the relationship into which they had entered, or purported to have entered, to be decided by the same tribunal. 
- The clause had to be construed in accordance with that presumption unless the language made it clear that certain questions were intended to be excluded from the arbitrator's jurisdiction.

#### Enka v Chubb [2020] UKSC 38
- appellant appealed against a decision that english law governed an arbitration agreement in a construction contract with the respondent
- appellant was a russian insurance company which had insured the owner of a power plant against fire damage
- the respondent turkish company had been a contractor involved in the construction of the plant 
- the construction contract did not contain a governing choice of law clause, but provided for disputes to be referred to english arbitration
- the plant was destroyed in a fire
- the appellant payed out under the policy and began russian proceedings against the respondent and others
- the respondent argued that the dispute fell within the arbitration agreement and vegan english arbitration proceedings
- an English court that had to decide which system of law governed the validity, scope or interpretation of an arbitration agreement had to apply the common law rules for determining the law governing contractual relations, which would be the law expressly or impliedly chosen by the parties or, in the absence of such choice, the law with which the arbitration agreement was most closely connected
- The proper approach for determining whether there had been a choice of law was to apply English law as the law of the forum. The court was required to interpret the contract as a whole, applying the ordinary English rules of contractual interpretation. English common law recognised the possibility that different parts of a contract might be governed by different laws, in particular an arbitration clause which was separable from the rest of the contract, but it was generally reasonable to assume that parties would intend or expect their contract to be governed by a single system of law.
