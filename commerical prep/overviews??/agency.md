### ==statutes==

#### Commercial Agents (Council Directive) Regulations 1993

- The Statutory Definition (Regulation 2)
	- To qualify as a "commercial agent," a party must be:
		1. **A self-employed intermediary:** (Note: This includes limited companies, but **excludes** direct employees).
		2. **Continuing authority:** Not a one-off transaction.
		3. **Negotiate/Conclude:** Authority to negotiate the sale/purchase of goods on behalf of and in the name of a principal.
	-  **Key Exclusions (Reg 2.1 – 2.3):**
		- **Internal parties:** Company officers, partners, or insolvency practitioners.
		- **Specific markets:** Commodity exchanges/markets (_W Nagel v Pluczenik_ — diamonds sold via DeBeers "sights" held to be a commodity market, so Regulations didn't apply).
		- **Gratuitous agents:** Must be for remuneration.
		- **Secondary/Ancillary activities:** If agency is not the primary function of the engagement (_Crane v Sky In-Home Service Ltd_).
		- **Distributors:** They act on their own behalf (buying/reselling), not on behalf of a principal.
-  Duties of the Parties (Reg 3 & 4)
	- The Regulations introduce a rare requirement of **"Good Faith"** into English commercial law.
	- **Agent’s Duties (Reg 3):** Must look after principal’s interests, act dutifully/in good faith, make "proper efforts" to negotiate, and keep the principal informed.
	- **Principal’s Duties (Reg 4):** Must act dutifully/in good faith, provide necessary documentation/information, and notify the agent if volume of business is expected to drop significantly.
	- **Mandatory Nature:** Most of these duties (especially regarding commission information) **cannot be excluded** by contract (Reg 5).
-  Remuneration (Reg 6–12)
	- **Default Rule:** If the contract is silent, the agent is entitled to "customary" commission or, if none exists, "reasonable" remuneration.
	- **Payment Timing:** Commission must be paid no later than the last day of the month following the quarter in which it became due.
- Termination of Agency (Reg 14–16)
	- **Conversion:** A fixed-term contract that continues to be performed after expiry automatically becomes an **indefinite**contract (Reg 14).
	- **Minimum Notice Periods (Reg 15):** * 1 month for the 1st year.
		- 2 months for the 2nd year.
        - 3 months for the 3rd year and beyond.
    - **Immediate Termination (Reg 16):** Permitted only for failure to perform contractual obligations or "exceptional circumstances."
- Post-Termination Payments (Regulation 17)
	- This is the most litigated area. Agents are entitled to a payment even if the contract just ends naturally.
	- **Valuation Method:** _Lonsdale v Howard & Hallam [2007]_:
	    - The UK rejected the French "two years' gross commission" rule.
	    - **Rule:** Compensation is based on the **market value** of the agency at the date of termination (i.e., what a hypothetical purchaser would pay for the "goodwill" of that specific agency). If the principal's business is failing (as in _Lonsdale_), the compensation may be very low.
- Restraint of Trade (Reg 20)**
	- Any clause restricting an agent's activities after termination must:
		1. Be in **writing**.
		2. Relate to the **same geographical area/customer group**.
		3. Relate to the **same kind of goods**.
		4. Last for a maximum of **two years**.

### ==cases==

#### The Eurymedon [1975] AC 154
- stevedores damaged goods - could they rely on an exclusion clause in the bill of lading to which they where third parties?
- issue of privity 
- unilateral contract
- carrier treated as acting as an agent for the stevedores when the bill of lading was signedf
#### Freeman and Lockyer v. Buckhurst Park Properties (Mangal) Ltd [1964] 2 QB 480
- director acted as a managing director without being a managing director 
- board knew he was acting in this way but did not intervene
- director, kapoor, hired the architects, the company then refused to pay them, arguing that kapoor had no authority to engage them
- DIPLOCK LJ then spoke generally about authority:
	- express actual authority
	- implied actual authority 
	- apparent (ostensible authority)
	- he also said this about actual authority more generally:
		- an actual authority is a legal relationship between principal and agent created by a consensual agreement to which they alone are parties To this agreement the [third party] is a stranger; he may be totally ignorant of the existence of any authority on the part of the agent. Nevertheless, if the agent does enter into a contract pursuant to the actual authority it does create contractual rights and liabilities between the principal and [the third-party].  

#### European Asian Bank v. Punjab & Sind Bank (No 2) [1983] 1 WLR 642
- punjab & sind bank was an indian bank with a london branch
- they authorised a company called computaprint to draw on a credit facility
- the london branch manager sent a telex to european asian bank about countaprints authority to deal with certain documents and transaction
- european asian bank relied on this 
- when things went wrong, it was denied that the london branch manager had authority to make representations about countaprint
- the textbook uses this to say
	- if an agent receives vague instructions, they have a duty to clarify them. if they dont, neither they not the principal can use it as an excuse
	- the bank manager recieved vague instructions on what he could communicate about countaprint

#### Waugh v. H B Clifford & Sons [1982] Ch 374
- firm of builders instructed solicitors to act on their behalf
- without the specifc instructions from the buolders, the solicitors negotiated a settlement
- builders refused to honour the settlement
- did the solicitors have authority? - yes they had implied authority, implicit through the builder/solicitor relationship

#### Watteau v. Fenwick [1893] 1 QB 346
- fenwick owned a hotel, employed a manager called humble to run it
- license for premises was therefore in humbles name, his name was also above the door (to the outside world it appeared that he owned it)
- fenwick gave humble express instrcutions to onlu buy certain goods (bottled ales and mineral waters) not cigars and bovril
- humble purchased cigars and bovril on credit from watteau
- when there was no payment, watteau sued the undisclosed principal (fenwick)
- The principal is liable for all the acts of the agent which are within the authority usually confided to an agent of that character, notwithstanding limitations as between the principal and the agent, put upon that authority
	- principal liable for acts of agent which are usual conduct in that type of agent/principal relationship

#### Graves v. Legg (1857) 2 H & N 210
- london merchant employed a liverpool broker to purchase wool
- by custom of the liverpool market, where a contract requires notice of shipment, it i susual that the nlitce be given ti the broker and then passed to the buyer
- broker failed to pass names of ships onto the defendants
- defendants argued they has neevr recoevd rpipoer notice and wehre therefore not bound to accept the wool they had purchased - the market had since crashed
- When a contract is made within a particular trade, the practices and customs of that trade inform the meaning and weight of the contractual terms; regardless of whether those customs are spelled out explicitly

#### Keighley, Maxsted & Co v. Durant [1901] AC 240
- Roberts, a corn merchant at Wakefield, was authorised by Keighley, Maxstead & CO to buy wheat
- Roberts failed to buy at the authorised price, and entered into an unauthorised contract with Durant, a corn merchant in London, to buy from him at a higher price
- The contract was made in Roberts' own name - but with undisclosed intention to be made out to him and Keighley, Maxstead & CO.
- Keighley, Maxstead & CO. agreed to ratify 
- Robert and the appellants failed to take delivery of the wheat
- Durant was forced to sell at a later date, at a loss and sued for the difference
-  a contract by an agent made in his own name does not require, and cannot be ratified
- ratification is only necessary where a contract is made which is not valid without ratification
- therefore, the amount payable for breach of contract should be made against the rouge agent Roberts, and not Keighley, Maxstead & CO.
- agency through ratification must have named or identifiable principals
- a contract which itself is incomplete without ratification, must needs be ratified
	- a contract incomplete without ratification is a contract which was made with the intention of it needing ratification. that is, made by an agent in the name of a third party
	- a contract which has been made in the name of an agent, is of itself complete and therefore cannot be ratified

#### Summers v. Solomon (1857) 7 E & B 879
- Jewellers shop managed by A 
- A orders jewellery to shop
- He steals it
- Solomon (D) did not want to pay Summers for the goods
- A, whilst under Ds employment, would have, or would reasonably be known to have "apparent authority"
	- Prior conduct of Solomon, representing A as somebody with apparent authority had created this "apparent authority"
- This would make D liable for payment for the goods ordered (on apparent authority to Lowes in Sussex, and subsequently stolen by A upon arrival) by A
-  representations made that the person(s) had authority to act as an agent create an "apparent authority"
- prior conduct in this case was sufficient 
- Erie J - "not the actual relationship but what the outside world think"



#### Armagas v. Mundogas SA (The ‘Ocean Frost’) [1986] AC 717
- 3 year charterback agreement agreed by vice president who said he had authority when he didnt - he could only authorise up to 1 year
- apparent authority cannot be created by the agent himself - representation must come from the principal
- from the book: a person employed in a particular position would be taken to have authority to conclude all such transactions as a person in that position normally has authority to conclude 
- the representation must be relied upon - must have induced the third party into the contract

#### Springer v. Great Western Railway  [1921] 1 KB 257
- must have had no opportunity to obtain instructions from the owner

#### Decro-Wall International SA v. Practitioners in Marketing Ltd [1971] 2 All ER 216
- french manurfacturing company produced self adhesive vinyl wall panels
- they appointed the defendants as their sole concessionaires in the uk
- no express provision was present for termination
- a notice terminating a contract which is given in breach of contract still takes effect
- agent can bring an action for damages for breach of contract, but cannot obtain an injuction to restrain the principal from terminating the agency in breach of contract
- as well as claiming damages, an agent can also seek an injunction to enforce by any negative stipulations in the contract of agency - such as using other agents to carry on the business contracted for

#### Martin-Baker Aircraft Co Ltd v. Canadian Flight Equipment Ltd [1955] 2 QB 556
- aircraft ejection seats
- wanted to expand into the US
- entered into two agreements
- one provided no termination clause
- the other required breach unremedied after 14 days notice or insolvecy 
- decided to terminate both contracts
- where there is no stipulations as to the length of notice to be given, it must just be reasonable
- reasonable is determined on the facts as they exist at the time the notice is given - not as the contract was made

#### Rhodes v. Forwood (1876) 1 App Cas 256
- engagment of agent to sell coal produced at the principals colliery
- agent acted for the principal for 4 years
- principal sold solliery and ceased to carry on business
- principal was under no obligation to compensate agent for loss of anticiapted comission
- there was no term, express or implied that the principal would caryy on business, or that the agent would be employed for a minimum period

#### Cunliffe-Owen v. Teather and Greenwood [1967] 3 All ER 561
- put options acquired through a stockbroker
- transactions were subject to the rules and regulations of the stock exchange
- dispute arose regarding whether the claimant was bound by a specific stock exchnage usuage that only members could declare options
- where a principal authprises agent to act in a particlaur market, the principal os generally bound by the established usgages or customs of that market, wheter the princiapl knew of them or not
- must be certain, notorious and reasonable
- agent must therefore act within the customs and usages of the market , unless expressely forbidden

#### Midland Bank Trust Co Ltd v. Hett, Stubbs and Kemp [1979] Ch 384
- solicitors failed to register an option to purchase a farm as a land charge 
- the option was subsequently defeated when the owner sold to a third party
- agent owes a duty to their principal to perform their mandate with reasonable care and skill
- agent may be concurrently liable to their principal in both contract and tort - breach of agencey agreement and negligence

#### Choudhry v. Prabhakar [1988] 3 All ER 718
- claimant asked friend to find a second hand car that had not been in an accident
- friedn acted as a gratuitous agent and recommened a car that seemed to be in good nick
- car had actually been in an accident and was unroadworthy
- agency can exist without payment
- even a gartuitous agent owes their principal a duty of care
	- standard is lower than a professional agent 
	- still must excerise degree of care and skill that could reasonably be expected of them in the circumstances - this is an objective duty of care
- agent can also be liable to principal for damages resulting from negligent misstatements upon which the principal relied


#### Allam & Co Ltd v. Europa Poster Services Ltd [1968] 1 All ER 826
- was a notice to terminate a contract, given by a solicitor (acting as agent) valid?
- the defendant company acting as an agent had delegated some of thei rdutuies to a sub agent, their solicitor
- delegatus non potest delegare
	- agent whos authority has been delegated cannot  sub delegated thta authority to another wihtout express or implied permission from the principal
- an authorized delegation by way of express actual authority, impled actual authority or usual authority or where the transaction is subsequently ratified by the principal, is not a breackj of theduty to act personally

#### McPherson v. Watt (1877) 3 App Cas 254
- solicitor acting as agent fro two ladies was tasked with finding a purchaser for their house
- advised them not to advertise and then purchased the house himself, nominally, in the name of his brother, while concealing his owne interst in the transaction
- agent has the duty to disclose interests to principal if there is a conflict etc
- even if the price paid is fair if an agent pourchases soemthign for themselves off the principal, if it is not disclosed, then it makes the contract voidable by the principal

#### Faccenda Chicken Ltd v. Fowler [1987] Ch 117
- sales manager and severla van salesman left employer to start a competing business
- they used sensitive sales infromation, such as customer names, routes and prices to solicit the ex employers customers
- during the term of agency (employemnt), an agent has an implied duty of good faith or fideltiy to their principal
- after an agencye ends, the agent is generally free to use the skill and general knoiwlegde acquired during the agency.
- however, they remain restricted from using specific trade secrects or highly confidential information that they were bound by  an iomplied term not to use or disclose
#### Boardman v. Phipps [1967] 2 AC 46
- solicitor to a trust and a benificary of that trust acted as self appointed agents to obtain information about a company in which the trust held shares.
- they were not satisifed with the company's performance and attendedc meetings as agemts for the trust, using their position to gain detailed internal knowledge about the company's assets.
- they used this info to personally purchase controlling interest in the company, which was highly profitable for themselves and the trust.
- a benificary sued for an account of the profits
- agents in a fidicuary position are strictly prohibited from mking profit from their position without fully informed consent of theri principal
- knowledge acquired in the course of agent business is a fiduciary asset. if they make profit from this, they must accoutn for that profit to the principal
- even self appointed agents have the same fiduciary duties
