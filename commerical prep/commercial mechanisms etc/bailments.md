

## what is a bailment? #card 
- somebody holding goods on behalf of somebody else



## who is the bailor? #card 
- the owner of the goods


## who is the bailee? #card 
- the one holding the goods


## what can be bailed? #card 
- only personal, tangible property


## what are the three ways in which bailments are created? #card 
- contract
- consent
- construction


## where are remedies found? #card 
- in contract
- in tort


## what can be bailed? #card 
- only personal property (not real property)
- must be tangible (cannot be a chose in action)
- valuable paper can be bailed (share cert, insurance policy, pile of cash etc)


## what are the different types of bailment? #card
- depositum
- commodatum
- locatio et conductio
- vadium
- locatio operis faciendi
- mandatum
- locatio custodiae
- constructive bailments
- odd cases


## what is depositum? #card 
- goods delivered to another to keep without a fee
- Khan v Grocutt


## what is commodatum? #card 
- lending something for the use of the borrower, without charge
- Coughlin v Gillinson


## what is locatio et conductio? #card 
- hiring goods to another
- Chapman v GWR; Chapleton v Barry UDC


## what is vadium? #card 
- pledging goods 
- Donald v Suckling


## what is locatio operis faciendi? #card 
- goods delivered to another to be carried for hire/for something to be done to them
- Hollier v Rambler Motors



## what is mandatum? #card 
- good delivered to another to be conveyed, or for a particular purpose without hire/charge
- Coggs v Bernard



## what is locatio custodiae #card
- what would otherwise be a depositum but for reward, ie where goods are bailed to someone who is paid to look after them, also known as hire of custody


## what is a constructive bailment? #card 
- includes non consensual bailments such as theft



## what odd case bailments are there? #card 
- conditional sales
- railway tickets issued on condition of surrender at end of your journey 
- etc



## where can remedy be sought? #card 
- negligence
- contract



## negligence #card
- duty of care established through bailment
- standard of care is reasonable care
- exceptions:
	- no defence of *jus tertii*
	- in locatio operis faciendi, no requirment to even prove negligence - Chapman v. Great Western Rly
	- liable for crimes of third parties - BRS v Arthur V Crutchley Ltd
	- liable for actions of servants/contractors - BRS v Arthur V Crutchley Ltd, Port Swettenham Authority v. TW Wu & Co
	- sub bailees can be sued directly - the pioneer container
	- normal burden of proof is reversed - Travers & Sons Ltd v. Cooper



## contract #card
- normal contract rules apply



## trover #card 
- dealing with goods in a way that **denies the owner's title**
	- abuse of possession
	- refusal to deliver goods when demanded
- must have right to possession
- Damage must be proved
- About the **rights over goods**, not just physical interference
- damages and remedies *in rem*



## Torts (Interference with Goods) Act 1977 #card
- loss or destruction of goods which a bailee has allowed to happen in breach of duty to bailor
- remedies:
	- Delivery of the goods plus damages
    - Delivery of goods with no damages
    - Damages alone (value of goods + any consequential loss)


## trespass #card 
- Requires **direct** interference with goods
- Actionable **per se** (no need to prove damage)
- Can be unintentional
- must have right to possession
- damages
