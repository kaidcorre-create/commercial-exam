### brief overview


- **sources of sales law**
	- case law
	- sales by traders to consumers - Consumer Rights Act 2015
	- Unfair Contract Terms Act 1977
	- sale of goods act 1979
		- case law predating the 1893 act is often used to aide statutory interpretation
	- sale of goods act 1893 - later replaced by 1979 act, lots of similarities, just updated the law to better reflect the times - first US sales acts modelled after it (Louisiana is still)
			- "an act for codifying the law relating to the sale of goods", "to present an orderly and authoritative statement of the leading rules of law on a given subject, whether they are to be found in stature law or common law".
			- made from all relevant case law at the time, as well as consultations with those that it will effect
			- Sir Mackenzie Chalmers did it
			- largely concerned with business to business
			- attempted to be a one size fits all model for all contracts
- **principal definitions**
	- goods
		- defined in [section 61 of the sale of goods act 1979](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1979/54/section/61) as "all personal chattels other than things in action and money"
			- includes emblements and industrial growing crops - (==what if they spoil?==)
			- things attached to the land, which are agreed to be severed before sale (importantly not contract formation)
			- money as collectors items are goods and not things in action - gold coins etc
			- NOT ANY OF THESE things in action - choses in action - legal right over things, enforced by an action
				- shares in a company
				- court judgement for damages
				- money
				- bonds
	- sale
		- define in [section 2 (1) of the sale of goods act 1979](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1979/54/section/2) transfer of ownership from seller to buyer in exchange for a money considerations (or part money consideration) - known as the price
		- (4) Where under a contract of sale the property in the goods is transferred from the seller to the buyer the contract is called a *sale*.
		- (5) Where under a contract of sale the transfer of the property in the goods is to take place at a future time or subject to some condition later to be fulfilled the contract is called an *agreement to sell*.



### passing of property
- significance of property
	- s.2 SGA 1979 - agreeing to transfer the property in goods to the buyer
	- what does property mean?
		- s.61 defines it as general property in goods and not merely a special property
		- transferring ownership - bundle of rights (might be subject to third party interests)
		- not the same as title
			- a title is good against the whole world - property may have 3rd party interests (hire purchase sales etc)
			- property is good against the seller - seller has no claim as sold/transferred rights
			- the property agreed to be transferred may include a good title - but it may not
		- 
	- dependant on passing of proerty:
		- risk
			- goods are at the risk of whoever owns them - whoever has property of them. risk passes with property (unless contracted not to)
			- if damage occurs before the passing of property, the risk is with the seller (if made to order)
				- but if buying a specific item - rather than made to order, the risk passes when the contract is made
			- s.20 SGA 1979
				- unless otherwise sgreed, the good reamin at tht esellers risk until the property in trasnffered to the buyer
				- but when the property is trasnffered, the goods are at the buyers risk depending on whether or not delivery has been made or not
				- but where goods are delayed by the fault or either the buyer or seller, the goods are at the risk of the party at fault
		- basis of action
			- sellers rights
				- s.49 SGA 1979
					- property passed = action for the price
						- where there is a contract of sale, the property in the goods has passed to the buyer and he wrongfully negelcts or refuses to pay for the goods acording to the terms of the contract, the seller may maintian an action against him for the price of the goods
						- where the price is payable on a given day, the seller may maintain an action for the price, although the property in the goods has not passed and the goods have not been appropriated to the contract
					- property has not passed = s.50 SGA 1979
						- the seller may maintain action for damages for non acceptance
						- measure of damages is based on direct loss
						- where there is a market for the goods, the measure of damages is prima facie to be ascertained bu the difference in contract price and tje market price at the time when the goods ought to have been accepted
