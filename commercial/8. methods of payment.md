introduction
- payment is essential to commercial life
	- enables continuity of business operations
	- prevents insolvency risk s for sellers and service providers
	- protects payer from default, enforcement actions and insolvency processes
	- beyond a factual transfer, it is a legal act with consequences
- the method of payment matters:
	- different methods have different legal effects ON:
		- discharge of payment obligations
		- transferability and circulation in commerce - some can be transferred, other cannot
		- title and risk allocation
		- circulation of value is central to commercial life
- documentary payments
	- legal documents accepted for partial or full discharge of a payment obligation
	- negotiable instruments. eg a cheque 
	- see Bills of Exchange Act 1882
		- sets out all of the steps for it to be valid
- transferability v negotiability
	- transferability:
		- ability to pass a payment instrument from one person to another so the recipient acquires the same rights as the transferor
	- negotiability:
		- a higher form of transferability where the person receiving the instrument may gain a better title than the transferor, meaning the prior defects in ownership do not follow the instrument
- nemo dat quod non habet
	- negotiable payment methods create an exception to this rule
	- a bona fide purchaser for value without notice is a holder in due course s.29 of Bills of Exchange Act 1882
	- Transferees title is unaffected by defects in transferors title
	- transferable does not equal negotiable (cross cheques under s.81 Bills of Exchange Act 1882)
- Documentary intangible
	- physical property carrying significant tangible property rights
		- bills of exchange 
		- promissory notes
	- paper money is an asset, however it carries more significant value than the paper on which it is printed
- things in action/chose in action
	- actions
		- intangible property enforceable only through legal action, not by physical possessions
- proprietary right
	- right over property, enforceable against the world
- personal rights
	- rights enforceable only against a specific right
- fungible
	- substitute but substantively identical chattel constitutes valid performance of obligation


meat and bones
- what constitutes a a payment?
	- performance or discharge of a money obligation
		- arises under commercial agreements
		- Sale of Goods Act s.2(2), s.(28)
		- barter is not a payment
- who must make the payment?
	- normally debtor
	- payment is still valid if made by someone
		- with authority
		- if its later ratified by debtor
			- but voluntary payments (unratified/unreimbursed by debtor to third party) do not establish a right of reimbursement [[Owen v Tate [1976] ]]
- requirement of acceptance by creditor
	- payment must be accepted by the creditor
	- this can be implied 
	- contractual terms may dictate method of payment
	- payment obligation still stands if not accepted
- prescription of method of payment
	- if method is specified, payment must follow the agreed method
	- if no method is specified, the creditor can accept or reject (if unreasonable)
	- third party payments are valid if accepted by the creditor
		- risk still exists
		- payment obligation not discharged if intermediary defaults
- valid tender vs legal tender
	- valid tender
		- payment method creditor accepts
	- legal tender
		- protects debtor against action for non payment if paid into court
	- Coinage Act 1971, s.2
- payment methods
	- part exchange 
		- combines money and goods to satisfy payment obligation
			- full goods would be barter
	- novation
		- replaces an existing obligation with aa new contract and discharges old one
		- common in banking, restructuring loans or debt payments
	- money
		- medium of exchange 
		- unit of account
		- store of value
		- types
			- fiat/physical money
				- legal tender - discharges payment obligation unless contract specifies otherwise
				- cannot be bought or exchanged
				- physical chattel
				- fungible
				- thing/chose in possession
					- ownership passes on delivery to a person giving value (chose in action is a right to enforce and legal action must be taken to enfoirce)
			- electronic money
				- monetary value is stored electronically and can be used for payment transaction
				- electronic monies relates to a stored monetary value which represents amount that can be claimed against the bank
				- like a promise to pay the numbers on the screen
				- e money represents claim on issuer, nit a direct ownership of the money
				- emoney is not transferable or negotiable like fiat money
			- crypto/virtual
				-  functions as a medium of exchange and/or unit of account and/or store of value
				- it is not legal tender, nor is it issued or guaranteed by a state
				- it fulfils its function only by agreement by its community of users
			- payments
				- funds transfer
					- money transferred between bank accounts
					- invloves debit and credit without actual transfer of money
					- no money stored anywhere
					- BACS
						- bankers automated clearing service
						- handles debits/credits
						- 3 working days to clear
						- lower value transactions - often used for regular payments
						- payment instructions can be revoked if the payer requests it before 3:30pm on the second clearing day
					- CHAPS
						- clearing house automated payment system
						- same day payments - facilitates real time payments
						- typically higher value
						- used in commercial transactions where timing is important
						- payments cannot be revoked one the payers bank had processed the transaction
				- electronic payments
					- payments via devices, cards or apps
					- banks act as agent for both payor and payee
			- 
- documentary payments
	- developed overtime to facilitate trade
	- functions as a cash substitute
	- method of payments embodied in legal documents known as instruments
	- it is unconditional promise or order to pay a fixed sum
	- electronic payments reduced the use of traditional payment methods
		- still remain relevant in
			- high value transactions
			- international trade
	- negotiable instrument
		- can be transferred by delivery alone or by delivery and indorsement
			- s.31 Bills of Exchange Act 1882
			- indorsement meaning signed over on the document
		- transfer passes legal title without further process, no notice required
		- exception to nemo dat rule
			- a bona fide purchaser/transferee acquires a good title even if the transferors title was defective
			- may be circumstances where title cant pass
		- two main forms of negotiable instruments
			- promise to pay (promissory note)
				- i promise to pay x amount on x date 
					- cannot be conditional
				- drawer -> payee -> negotiated third part
				- drawer promises to pay; negotiable to a third party (unlimted times until the date)
				- s.83 Bills of Exchange Act 1882
			- order to pay (bill of exchange)
				- is an order to someone to pay somebody else
				- drawer -> drawee (bank) ->payee
				- drawer orders bank to pay, payment occurs on presentation
				- s.3 & s.73 of Bill of Exchange Act 1882
		- promissory note - promise to pay - s.83
		- bill of exchange - order to pay - s.3
			- must be an unconditional order in writing
			- must be addressed by one person to another
			- must be signed by the person giving it
			- must be on demand or at a fixed date or determinable future time
			- a specified sum in money
			- payable to a specified person or bearer
			- a bill is not invalidated if s.3(4):
				- it is not dated
				- does not specify value given (the consideration)
				- does not state place of drawing or payment
				- a bill can be  incomplete or formally irregular when issues
					- under s.20 a bill can be completed by the holder
					- completion is binding on previous holder if completed within a reasonable time and strictly in accordance with the authority given
					- under s.20(2) this does not apply to a holder in due course
						- they take the bill free from defects both in relation to authority and completeness s.29
						- s.29(2) - defective if obtained by fraud, duress, illegality, breach of faith
		- cheque - type of bill of exchange - s.73
- discounting of bills
	- bills of exchange can be transferred before maturity 
	- it allows the holder to convert future cash into immediate cash
	- some bills are non negotiable and non trasnferrable
		- account payee cheque - has the payees name on it - s.81
	- some bills and non negotiable
		- they can be transferred but transferees cannot take a better title than transferors
- order bills vs bearer bills
	- order bills - written to someones name, can be endorsed
		- must sign the back of it
		- if the payee is fictitious or non existent then it will be treated as a bearer bill
	- bearer bills - to be paid to whoever bears the bill
		- less secure, easier to circulate
- liability of parties under a bill
	- drawee/acceptor
		- primary obligator must pay the bill once accepted s.54 BEA 1882
		- cannot avoid liability by facts against drawer/payee
		- a draws a bill on b. b accepts and therefore must pay on maturity
	- drawer/payee
		- guarantees bill will be honoured s.55(1)
		- compensates holder/indorser if dishonoured
		- a draws a bill on b for x amount. be refuses, a pays holder c
	- indorser
		- liable if order bill or beaer bill indorsed s.55(2), s.58(2)
		- subsequent holder can claim if bill is dishonoured
		- c indorses to d. bill dihonoured. c pays d
	- exclusion of liability 
		- parties may limit their liability under s.16 "without recourse" - order bill
		- only signatories are liable under s.23 - bearer bill
			- non signatories cannot be pursued
- key defences
	- forgery or unauthorised signature - nullity
	- s,24 - a forged or unauthorised signature wholly inoperative
		- no liability can arise from it - no valid title passes through forgery
	- lack of capacity. s.22
	- material alteration
		- s.64 - any change to essential terms (unless all liable parties consent to alteration)
	- s.59-64 - payment at maturity or lawful discharge terminates liability
- hierarchy of liability 
	- holder/payee - if unpaid/refuses
	- indorsers - later indorser can claim from earlier one (each liable to the next)
	- drawer - ordered payment and liable if drawee refuses
	- drawee/acceptor - primary party must pay
