### what is a bailment?
- somebody else holding goods on the behalf of somebody else
	- carriage of goods
	- sales awaiting collection
	- pawnbrokers
	- warehousing
	- hire purchase
- bailor - owner
- bailee - one holding goods
- goods subject to a bailment are said to be bailed by the bailor to the bailee

### nature of a bailment
- a separate obligation alongside contracts, torts and restitution
- can be created contractually, consensually, constructively or consensually
- duties can be contractually defined and/or as found in tort (duties) - a contract does not lie at the heart of a bailment - a bailment does
	- remedies can therefore be sort in contract or in tort
- remedies can be damages or *in rem*
- special rules apply as tot he burden of proof

### what can be bailed?
- only personal property - not real property (land etc)
- must be tangible - cannot be a chose (thing) in action
- valuable paper can be bailed (share certificate, insurance policy doc, pile of real cash money)
- comparable to goods as defined in Sale of Goods Act 1979 - but there are distinctions

### liability in negligence and bailee's duty of care - suing in negligence
- bailee is responsible for actions of servants - vicarious liabililty
- theft
	- [[British Road Services v Arthur V Crutchley Ltd]] - normally no libaility in negligence for the crimes of a third party, but the rule is otherwise for a bailee where the property bailed to him is stolen
- servants
	- [[Port Swettenham Authority v T W Wu & Co]] - a bailee is responsoble for the actipns of its servants
- independent contractors
	- [[British Road Services v Arthur V Crutchley Ltd]] - unlike ordinary negligence cases, the bailee is responsible for the actions and negligence of the independent contractors it engages
- sub-bailees
	- each bailee is liable for each sub-bailee - no matter how far down the chain they are. bailees/sub-bailees can add each other as 3rd parties or sue to recover loss 
	- each bailee and sub-bailee down the chain can be sued, or a bailee higher in the chain can be sued for actions/negligence of their sub-bailees
	- [[The 'Pioneer' Container]] - ran into another ship in high fog - goods transported lost. there was a choice of defendant. clause in original contract of carriage afforded protection to bailees and to sub bailee on any terms. sub bailed on favourable terms to sub-bailee in terms of exclusion of liabillity and so nobody was liable. had the clause not been in the original contract, then there wad no protection afforded
	- exclusion clauses apply
- no defence of *jus tertti*
	- [[Biddle v Bond]] - bailees are estopped from pleading *jus tertii* against their bailor or any bailor further up the chain. - bailee cannot deny the bailor's title (legal title of property) 
- burden of proof is on defendant bailee
	- normal burden of prof reversed - prove that they were not negligent (true)
	- [[Travers (Joseph) & Sons Ltd v Cooper]]
	- normal standard of care as appropriate to the goods is applied

Frodo, who has recently taken over a house (“Bag End”) from his uncle Bilbo, wishes to stamp his personality on the drawing-room.  The furniture is good-quality and he does not want to get rid of it, but he decides that the rather shabby 111 year-old carpet could do with replacing.  He finds nothing he likes in the shops of Hobbiton or Michel Delving and so sets out for a shopping trip to the more distant village of Bree.  At Bree Carpet & Curtain Warehouse (‘Bree Carpets’) he finds a large one-off handmade rug which will suit the room perfectly and at once agrees to buy it.  As he has only a small pony for transport, he arranges to leave the carpet with the shop until it is collected by Gandalf, a travelling magician, who will be passing through fairly soon to perform at Frodo’s housewarming party at Bag End.  A few days later Bree Carpets takes a large delivery of new stock and the manager decides that some of the existing stock will need to be stored elsewhere.  He speaks to Barliman Butterbur, the owner of the Prancing Pony Inn, who has plenty of space in his outbuildings and agrees to take the stock, including Frodo’s rug.

Gandalf is delayed in coming by an urgent appointment with Saruman.  In the meantime, adverse climatic conditions caused by heavy black clouds from the direction of Mordor, cause Butterbur’s beer to spoil.  He contacts the Golden Perch, a brew-pub in the nearby Shire, and they agree to sell him replacement stock, but only for cash on delivery.  Butterbur is beside himself, as he has no cash, but is approached by Bill Ferny, a local villain, and regular at the Prancing Pony, who offers the cash but demands security.  Butterbur, who has no security to offer, lets Ferny take some of Bree Carpets’ stock from his outhouse as security instead.  Ferny promptly sells these to Mr and Mrs Barrow-Wight, who do not know that Ferny is not the owner.

The next day, Strider, an undercover policeman, warns Butterbur (who does not know of Strider’s real identity) that he should take special precautions, as there are ‘some very dubious characters around, intent on mischief and destruction’.  The following day, The Black Riders, a motorcycle gang, arrive in Bree and pull up at the Prancing Pony.  Because they refuse to remove their black-visored helmets, Butterbur refuses to serve them and they ride off, vowing unspecified vengeance.  That very night, long after closing time, a handful of the Black Riders sneaks in at the unlocked back gate of the Prancing Pony and sets light to the outhouses, destroying the remaining stock, which includes Frodo’s rug.

A few days later, just to cap things, Butterbur is caught by the police serving drinks after hours and the Prancing Pony’s licence is suspended.  Butterbur is unable to trade, and so unable to pay Ferny.

**Advise:**

**(1)** **Frodo – who wants his carpet;**

**(2)** **Barliman Butterbur – from whom Frodo is demanding his carpet; and**

**(3)** **Mr and Mrs Barrow-Wight, who love their new carpet.**



### (1) Advice to Frodo – who wants his rug back

Frodo originally purchased the rug from Bree Carpets and took title to it. He then left it with Bree Carpets for safe-keeping until Gandalf could collect it. This created a **bailment for the mutual benefit of bailor and bailee** (Frodo gets storage and later delivery; Bree Carpets keeps the custom and goodwill of a satisfied customer). A bailee for reward (or mutual benefit) owes a duty to take reasonable care of the goods and is liable for loss or damage caused by its negligence. - depositum

**Chain of events and successive bailments**

1. **Bree Carpets → Butterbur**  
   Bree Carpets sub-bailed the rug (along with other stock) to Butterbur’s outhouses. A bailee may sub-bail if it is customary or reasonably necessary and the sub-bailee knows (or ought to know) the goods belong to a third party. Here it was reasonably necessary because of lack of space, and there is no evidence Butterbur was told the individual items belonged to customers. The sub-bailment was therefore valid, and Butterbur became a **gratuitous bailee for the benefit of Bree Carpets** (and indirectly for Frodo). A gratuitous bailee is only liable for gross negligence.

2. **Butterbur → Bill Ferny**  
   Butterbur then allowed Ferny to take some of the stored stock (not including Frodo’s rug) as security for a loan. This was an unauthorised dealing with the bailed goods. Butterbur is in breach of the bailment and is liable to Bree Carpets (and therefore indirectly to Frodo) for conversion of whatever goods Ferny took. However, **Frodo’s rug was not one of the items Ferny took**, so this breach does not directly cause the loss of Frodo’s rug.

3. **Destruction by fire**  
   The rug that remained in the outhouse was destroyed when the Black Riders set fire to it.

**Liability of the bailees for the fire**

- **Butterbur’s duty**: As a gratuitous bailee he is only liable for gross negligence. Leaving the back gate unlocked at night in Bree, even after Strider’s vague warning about “dubious characters”, is probably ordinary negligence at worst. There is no evidence of gross negligence or recklessness. Butterbur is therefore **not liable** to Bree Carpets (or to Frodo) for the fire.
- **Bree Carpets’ duty**: Bree Carpets was a bailee for reward/mutual benefit and owed Frodo a duty of reasonable care. By sub-bailing to a gratuitous bailee (Butterbur) who only owed a lower standard of care, Bree Carpets exposed Frodo’s rug to a higher risk. Moreover, Bree Carpets never warned Butterbur that some of the stock belonged to customers rather than to the shop itself. In these circumstances Bree Carpets has **failed to take reasonable care** of Frodo’s rug and is liable to him in bailment for its value (Mitchell v Ealing LBC [1979] QB 1; applied in the carpet-storage cases).

**Frodo’s practical position**

Frodo has a straightforward claim in bailment (or alternatively in contract/tort) against **Bree Carpets** for the full value of the rug. He has no direct claim against Butterbur because there was no privity of bailment between them and Butterbur was not grossly negligent. Frodo’s title is good against the rest of the world except a buyer in market overt (abolished) or a buyer protected by an exception to nemo dat (none applies here).

Frodo should therefore demand the value of the rug from **Bree Carpets**. If they refuse, he can sue them.

### (2) Advice to Barliman Butterbur – from whom Frodo is demanding the rug

Butterbur’s position is strong:

- He was a gratuitous bailee and the fire was not caused by his gross negligence.
- Frodo has no direct bailment claim against him.
- His only breach (allowing Ferny to take some stock) did not cause the loss of Frodo’s particular rug.

Butterbur can safely tell Frodo that he must look to Bree Carpets, with whom Frodo dealt and who chose to store the rug on Butterbur’s premises without warning him of its true ownership.

Butterbur may himself have a claim against Bree Carpets for the value of any of **his own** stock that Ferny took or that was destroyed, but that is a separate matter.

### (3) Advice to Mr and Mrs Barrow-Wight – who love their new carpet

The Barrow-Wights bought one of the rugs that Bill Ferny took from Butterbur’s outhouse. That rug belonged to **Bree Carpets** (it was their stock, not a customer’s rug like Frodo’s).

Ferny had no title and no authority to sell it. The sale took place privately, not in market overt (which no longer exists anyway), and none of the statutory exceptions to nemo dat (Factors Act, Hire-Purchase Act s.27, Sale of Goods Act s.25, etc.) apply on these facts.

Title therefore remains with **Bree Carpets**. The Barrow-Wights obtained no title and Bree Carpets can recover the rug (or its value) from them in conversion. The fact that the Barrow-Wights were innocent purchasers for value makes no difference in English law in a private sale.

The Barrow-Wights’ only remedy is against Bill Ferny for breach of the implied warranty of quiet possession / freedom from encumbrances under s.12 Sale of Goods Act 1979, but Ferny is a villain who has probably long since disappeared.

Unfortunately the Barrow-Wights will have to return their lovely new carpet to Bree Carpets (or pay its value).

### Summary

- Frodo recovers the value of his rug from **Bree Carpets**.
- Butterbur is not liable to anyone for Frodo’s rug.
- The Barrow-Wights lose their rug to **Bree Carpets**.
