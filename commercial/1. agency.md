## ==key features

- agency makes a contract legally enforceable between parties who would have otherwise not been privy to the contract
- [[Watteau v Fenwick]] - "the principal is liable for all the acts of the agent which are within the authority usually confided to an agent of that character, notwithstanding limitations as between the principal and the agent, put upon authority"
	- [[Graves v Legg]] - authority to act in accordance with trade customs, ignorance of principal to these is irrelevant
		- BUT must be notorious, certain and reasonable *(Perry v Barnett)*


## ==creation of agency 
- ==**by agreement** 
	- [[Heard v Pilley]] - no special formalities required
	- ==express== 
		- contract (normal rules apply)
	- ==implied ==
		- [[Waugh v HB Clifford & Sons]] - (can be through relationships wife, solicitor etc.). it is implicit in the normal workings/nature of the job/relationship that there is a level of implied agency
		- authority can be implied through the conduct of the principal/agent
	- ==usual==
		- [[Watteau v Fenwick]] - through custom/the usual way of doing business
- ==**through ratification**
	- agreement can be made subject to ratification. if it not, then, even if the thrid party withdraws their offer, they will still be bound if the principal later ratifies - [[Bolton partners v Lambert]].
		- ratification is the real acceptance is offer made subject to ratification - [[Warehousing & Forwardign Co of East Africa Ltd v Jafferali & Sons lts ]]
	- three conditions make ratification possible 
		- the grant must purport to act for a named or idenfitiable principal
		- the principal must have actually existed and been ascertainable at the time the contract was made
		- the principal must have the necessary capacity to make that contract
	- [[Keighley, Maxstead & CO v Durant]] - named or identifiable principals
	- [[Kelner v Baxter]] - principal must exist and be ascertainable when contract was made
		- promoters of an unformed company entered contracts on its behalf and argued that the comoany had ratified the contracts when it came into existence
		- equity gets around this fro peole setting up companies in that they can be treated as trustees fro the company (only if payment or promise of payment was made)
		- Contract (Rights of Third Parties) Act 1999 also gets around this but imposese no liability on the company itself
	- [[Firth v Staines]] - principal must have capacity at the time of the contract formation
	- ratification must also be:
		- timely 
		- nullities cannot be ratified
		- the prinicpal must have full knowledge of the facts
		- ratification can be by words or conduct but need not be communicated to the third party
	- [[Re Portuguese Consolidated Copper Mines]] - must be ratified in a timely manner
	- [[Danish Mercantile Co v Beaumont]] - null contracts cannot be ratified
		- void in law, not merely voidable
	- [[Forman & Co v The Ship Liddesdale]] - ratification must be done with a full knowledge of all the facts
	- [[The Kilmun]] - can be communicated by words or conduct, no need to notify the third party
	- ratification cannot be done by an undisclosed principal
- ==**ostensible or apparent authority - parties estopped by the law of equity from denying that agency**
	- there are four things that would be present here:
		- a representation must have been made that the person had authority to act as an agent
		- the representation must be one of fact, not law
		- the representation must have been made by the principal to the third party
		- the third party must have relied on the representation
	- however, even if these are all present, authority may be vitiated where a forger has been held out as having authority to conduct the type of transaction in question - [[Uxbridge Permanent Benefit Building Society v. Pickard [1939] 2 KB 248. ]]
	- And even when the ingredients are not present, a principal may be estopped by his own subsequent conduct from denying the agent’s authority if the third party has relied on that conduct - [[Spiro v. Lintern [1973] 3 All ER 319.]]  
	- 
	- [[Summers v Solomon]] - representation made that the person had authority to act as an agent
	- [[Kleinwort Benson v Lincoln City Council]] - representation of fact, not law (misunderstanding as to the legal effect of a statement)
	- [[The Ocean Frost]], [[Dickinson v Valpy]] - made by the principal to the third party, or a class including third party
	- [[The Ocean Frost]], [[Jacobs v Morris]] - reasonably relied upon by the third party
- ==**necessity**
	- that courtier case woth spolied grain
	- [[Prager v Blatspiel]], [[Stamp v Heacock Ltd]], [[Couturier v Hastie]] - only practicable action in the circumstances
	-  [[Springer v Great Western Railway]] - carrier or master must have had no oppoortunity at the time to get instructions from the owner
	- must have acted honestly and in the owners interest


## ==termination of agency==
- consensual
	- performance 
		- where the agency is for a particular purpose and this has been performed the agent is *functus officio* and the agency automatically ends - [[Blackburn v Scholes]] (but may be problems with ostensible authority if people still think agent has authority)
	- fixed term expires
		- either an agreed period or a period arriveed at by necessary implication or by a trade usuage or local custom - [[Dickinson v Lilwal]]
	- notice
		- notice can be given in a way different to the way the agency was origionanlly agreed
			- the point is that it does not need to be in any particular form
		- notice can be given either way
		- it is effective even if a breach on contract arises. the innocent party can claim damages but not an injunction, except in relation to negative stipulations - [[Decro-Wall International SA v Practitioners in Marketing Ltd]]
	- inconsistent conduct
		- the principal may sell the goods teh agent was mean to sell - [[Nelson v Rolfe]]
	- insanity 
		- either party automatically terminates the agency. - even when the agent does not know about the insanity - [[Yonge v Toynbee]]
	- death of either party
		- through an irrevocable power of attorney may still habe effect and bind the estate
	- dissolution of a compnay or partnership
	- cessation or transfer of business 
		- but where there is a fixed term, the agent may be entitled to compensation for lost future commission - [[Turner v Goldsmith]]
		- [[Rhodes v Forwood]]
	- bankruptcy of the principal
		- mutual accounts unaffected
	- bankruptcy of the agent
		- only if it affects the agents ability to perform duties
		- or if there is an express term to say that agency is terminated
- non consensual - usual or ostensible
	- termination must be notified of to third parties (with whom the agent deals) - [[Summers v Solomon]]
	- principal's insanity terminates actual authority, but not apparent unless the thrid party has notice - [[Drew v Nunn]]
	- agencies of necessity can be prevented by prior npotice to the agent - sometimes not effective - [[Great Northern Railway v Swaffield]]
- non termionable agencies
	- agency coupled with an interest - if authority given by deed or for valuable consideration and the purpose of creation is to protect an independent interest of the agent not connected with the agencey itself (so does not protect an agents interest in recieveing cmmmission nor does it protect a subsequently acquired intertest )
	- where the agent has become personally liable, or stands to suffer a loss other than loss of the commission - [[Seymour v Bridge]]
	- Irrevocable or enduring power of attorney - non issue in commercial cases



## ==duties of agents - common law
- **to perform the contract**
	- [[Turpin v. Bilton]], [[Cohen v. Kittell]]
		- in [[Turpin v. Bilton]], agent contracted to arrange insurance, didnt do so and was therefore liable when the ship sank uninsured
		- in [[Cohen v. Kittell]], not liable for failure to perform an illegal act, nor obliged to effect a contract which would be a nullity - the case concerned gambling but this is now not a nullity cause of ss334 and 335 of gambling act 2005
	-  failure to comply by the agreement (between agent and principle) gives rise to action for breach of contract. - *normal contract rules apply*
	- not obliged to perform illegal acts or effect a contract which will result in nullity
	- in absence of a contract, no liability for non performance, as no obligation (non-feasance) - [[Coggs v Bernard]] (the obligation arises from contract itself)
		- but could still be liable for mis-feasance where part performance causes loss or otherwise - *negligence (it is an exception to the rule of pure economic loss)* - [[Wilkinson v Coverdale]]
- **to be obedient to the principal**
	- agent must act in accordance with terms of authority granted by the principal
	- where do detail exists  - must act in accordance with general nature (*implied authority*).
	- [[Cunliffe-Owen v. Teather and Greenwood]] - must also act within the customs and usages of the market, unless expressly forbidden - (usual authority)
	- [[Volkers v Midland Doherty]] - must act in the best interests of the principal, that is to say, for the benefit of the principal. must not disregard express instructions in order to do so.
	- [[Bexwell v Christie]] - not required to act illegally
		- auctioneer was not liable to the origional owner of the goods diposed of at auction at a price lower than at that which the owner was prepared to sell. the auction was a sale without reserve, the autioneer was obliged by law to sell to the highest bidder
- **to exercise due care and skill**
	- [[Chaudry v Prabhakar]] - objective duty of care 
	- would be liable for breach of contract and tort of negligence
	- [[Lambert v Heath]] - standard of care is genrally that of the normally competent and careful agent conducting the sort of business in question
- **not to delegate**
	- [[Allam & Co v Europa Poster Services]]
- **not to deny the principal's title**
	- [[Dixon v Hamond]] - an agent must not deny his principal's title to goods dealt with by an agent
	- this applied even if the agent is entitled to the property
	- [[Biddle v Bond]] - BUT may refuse delivery to principal on grounds of *[[glossary|jus tertii]]* (third oarty claim)
- **to account**
	- must account properly
	- must hand over to the principal any monies he has received on behalf of the principal
	- this duty is absolute, even if a third party has a claim to money from the agent or the principal, and even if the principal owes the agent money - [[Heath v Chilton]]
	- it doesnt matter if teh contract under which the agent received the money from a third party was itself void or illegal [[De Mattos v Benjamin]]
	- doesnt apply where agent is obliged to return the money to the person he got it from
		- where the contract is void for mistake
		- and where the contract of agency is illegal 
		- [[Harry Parker Ltd v Mason]]

## ==duties of principals - common law
- **to remunerate** (if not gratuitous)
	- arises from contract/agreeement
	- agreed sum
	-  [[glossary|quantum meruit]] - [[Withy Robinson v Edwards]]
		- if no agreement is found as to the amount or basis of remuneration intended, then the agent is entitled to quantum meruit
	- commission 
		- if it can be shown that a comission basis was intended, then renumeration awarded will be a commission at a customary rate - [[Lewis and Graves v Harper]]
	- agent must EARN the remuneration
		- [[Green v Bartlett]] - it is a question of fact (as to whether or not remuneration has been earned)
			- need not actually effect the transaction, can just be the cause of it
		- the transaction need not actually benefit the principal - [[Fisher v Drewett]] (so long as the agent has actually done what they agreed to do and the lack of benefit is not a result of the failings of the agent)
	- subject to not having breached agent's duties
	- not payable on an illegal transaction ([[Haines v Busk]]) unless custom known to principal - [[Perry v Barnet]]
		- also wont be remunerated if transaction was effected in an unauthorised way, unless there is custom that he can recover and the principal knew of this custom [[marsh v Jelf]]
		- also cannot recover when negligent or in breach of other duties, such as a duty not to make secret profits [[Solomons v Pender]]
			- may still be recoverable if wrongdoing sepertaed from rest of agent's behaviour, so long as it was not fraudulent - [[Hippisley v Knee]]
- **to indemnify**
	- there will sometimes be express agreement on this matter, but it is usually implied. depends on circumstacnes - [[Adams v Morgan & Co]]
		- may also be excluded, if there is no contract then the existence and extent of the principals duty will depend upon the agent having acted within his express, implied or usual authority - [[Barron v Fitzgerald]]
	- entitled to indemnity of costs
	- lost where transaction is illegal, contract of agency is illegal, or agent is in breach of duty
	- can be excluded by a contractual term
	- if no contract, depends on agent having acted whin express , implied or usual authority 




## ==duties of commercial agent and principal - statutory==
- **Commercial Agents (Council Directive) Regulations 1993**
	- legislative creation, implementing an EU directive
	- part of retained EU law: s.25 European Union (Withdrawal Agreement) Act 2020
	- defined as (reg.2(1)) a  "self employed intermediary who has continuing authority to negotiate the sale or purchase of goods on behalf of another person ('the principal'), or to negotiate and conclude the sale or purchase of goods on behalf of and in the name if that principal"
	- A COMMERCIAL AGENT IS ONLY AN AGENT FOR THE SALE AND PURCHASE OF GOODS
- 3(1) requires agents to "look after the interests of his principal and acts dutifully in good faith"
- 3(2) agent must
	- "make proper efforts to negotiate and conclude the transactions"
	- keep the principal informed
	- comply with principals reasonable instructions
- (reg. 4(2)(b)). 
	- **to obtain all necessary information and communicate it to the agent **
- (reg. 4(2)(b)).
	- **to give reasonable notice to the agent if the volume of transactions is anticipated to be significantly lower than the agent could normally expect 
- (reg. 4(3))
	- **to give timely notification to the agent of any decision to accept or decline to execute any transaction procured by the agent 
- (reg. 10(3)).
	- **to pay commission promptly, that is, not later than the last day of the month following the quarter in which the commission became due 
	- **by the same date, to provide a statement of commission due, including the main components used in calculating the commission.**

COMMERICAL AGENTS
- **1. Legal Framework & Status**
	- **Source:** 
		- Originally an EU Directive; now **retained EU law** under s.25 European Union (Withdrawal Agreement) Act 2020.
	- **Purpose:** 
		- To protect agents, whom the EU views as "oppressed/vulnerable" compared to principals.
	- Scope:** 
		- Only applies to the **sale or purchase of GOODS**. It does not apply to services.
- **2. The Statutory Definition (Regulation 2)**
	- To qualify as a "commercial agent," a party must be:
		1. **A self-employed intermediary:** (Note: This includes limited companies, but **excludes** direct employees).
		2. **Continuing authority:** Not a one-off transaction.
		3. **Negotiate/Conclude:** Authority to negotiate the sale/purchase of goods on behalf of and in the name of a principal.
	-  **Key Exclusions (Reg 2.1 – 2.3):**
		- **Internal parties:** Company officers, partners, or insolvency practitioners.
		- **Specific markets:** Commodity exchanges/markets (_W Nagel v Pluczenik_ — diamonds sold via DeBeers "sights" held to be a commodity market, so Regulations didn't apply).
		- **Gratuitous agents:** Must be for remuneration.
		- **Secondary/Ancillary activities:** If agency is not the primary function of the engagement (_Crane v Sky In-Home Service Ltd_).
		- **Distributors:** They act on their own behalf (buying/reselling), not on behalf of a principal.
- **3. Duties of the Parties (Reg 3 & 4)**
	- The Regulations introduce a rare requirement of **"Good Faith"** into English commercial law.
	- **Agent’s Duties (Reg 3):** Must look after principal’s interests, act dutifully/in good faith, make "proper efforts" to negotiate, and keep the principal informed.
	- **Principal’s Duties (Reg 4):** Must act dutifully/in good faith, provide necessary documentation/information, and notify the agent if volume of business is expected to drop significantly.
	- **Mandatory Nature:** Most of these duties (especially regarding commission information) **cannot be excluded** by contract (Reg 5).
-  **4. Remuneration (Reg 6–12)**
	- **Default Rule:** If the contract is silent, the agent is entitled to "customary" commission or, if none exists, "reasonable" remuneration.
	- **Payment Timing:** Commission must be paid no later than the last day of the month following the quarter in which it became due.
- **5. Termination of Agency (Reg 14–16)**
	- **Conversion:** A fixed-term contract that continues to be performed after expiry automatically becomes an **indefinite**contract (Reg 14).
	- **Minimum Notice Periods (Reg 15):** * 1 month for the 1st year.
		- 2 months for the 2nd year.
        - 3 months for the 3rd year and beyond.
    - **Immediate Termination (Reg 16):** Permitted only for failure to perform contractual obligations or "exceptional circumstances."
- **6. Post-Termination Payments (Regulation 17)**
	- This is the most litigated area. Agents are entitled to a payment even if the contract just ends naturally.

| Feature      | **Compensation** (Default)                                  | **Indemnity** (Must be agreed in writing)                                  |
| ------------ | ----------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Logic**    | Reflects the loss of future commission.                     | Rewards the agent for "goodwill" handed over.                              |
| **Cap**      | **Unlimited** (no statutory ceiling).                       | **Capped** at 1 year’s average commission.                                 |
| **Criteria** | Agent must show loss of commission or unamortized expenses. | Agent must have brought new customers or increased business significantly. |

- **Valuation Method:** _Lonsdale v Howard & Hallam [2007]_:
    
    - The UK rejected the French "two years' gross commission" rule.
        
    - **Rule:** Compensation is based on the **market value** of the agency at the date of termination (i.e., what a hypothetical purchaser would pay for the "goodwill" of that specific agency). If the principal's business is failing (as in _Lonsdale_), the compensation may be very low.
        
-  **7. Restraint of Trade (Reg 20)**
	- Any clause restricting an agent's activities after termination must:
		1. Be in **writing**.
		2. Relate to the **same geographical area/customer group**.
		3. Relate to the **same kind of goods**.
		4. Last for a maximum of **two years**.







### seminar questions
- **Explain the doctrine of privity.  What inconveniences can it cause for commercial parties?**
	- the doctrine of privity suggests that a third party to a contract is unable to bring action for breach of said contract (or have action bought against them)
	- this may cause inconvenience to commercial parties, specifically in the case of [[Dunlop Pneumatic Tyre Co v Selfridges]] where it was stipulated that one is unable sell tyres atr a discounted rate to consumers. The problem that arose here, was that Selfridges had not sourced their tyres directly from Dunlop, and were therefore able to rely on this doctrine of privity as a defence; owing to the fact that they had not breached any contractual term, as no contract between the two parties existed.
- **How does the law on agency help with the problems you identified in answer to question 1?**
	- the law on agency can help circumvent the doctrine of privity by establishing an *agent* or *intermediary*, an example of which can be found in the case of [[The Eurymedon]].
		- in this case, there was a bill of lading for shipping a drill, a clause stipulated that action must be brought within one year, or there was no liability from the carriers for any damage caused; it was also stipulated that this should extend to any servants or agents of the carriers.
		- action was bought outside of one year against the stevedore, who claimed it was protected by this clause as a result of the doctrine of agency - that the carrier had made this contract on behalf of the stevedore
		- the shipper argued that privity of contract would apply in that the stevedore was a third party to the bill of lading
		- this case was decided in favour if the stevedore, in that the carrier had made a contract on behalf of the stevedore as an agent, initially unilateral but capable of becoming mutual. It became a full contract when the stevedore performed services (consideration) and the benefit of those services was in turn consideration passing from the shipper to the stevedore
