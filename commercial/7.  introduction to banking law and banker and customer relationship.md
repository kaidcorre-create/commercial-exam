customer
- no statutory definition in banking law
- referenced with no defintion in Bills of Exchange Act 1882, s 75(1)
- developed through case law
	- course of dealings - early approach 
	- account based relationship - modern law
		- once an account is opened, and the bank accepts to take deposits, they become a customer
		- no need for lots of dealings
	- Taxation of Commissioners v English, Scottish and Australian Bank [1920]
		- stolen cheque paid into an account opened by a thief for that purpose
		- legal qustion was is the theif a customer of the bank, the answer was yes
	- Ladbrook & Co v Todd [1914]
		- account opened by thief, impersonating someone else
		- impersonated person was not a customer
		- thief was a customer
	- fraud and customer
		- Marfani & Co Ltd v Midland Bank Ltd [1968]
			- fraudster oepened an account in the na,e of another person and deposited a a cheque
			- the court held that the fradster was the customer
		- Stoney Stanton Supplies (Coventry) Ltd v Midland Bank Ltd [1966]
			- fraudster opened account on behalf of company via forged signatures from company directors.
			- no banker-customer relationship existed 
			- difference between this and the above is mistake of quality??
			- difference is that the fraudster was frauduing oj behalf of a company, not pretednign to be an individual
- relationship begins once the bank agrees to open an account - Woods v Martins Bank [1959]
	- but Importers Co Ltd v Westminster Bank Ltd [1927], the bank may be a customer of another bank
- nature of the relationship between customers and banks
	- contractual, debtor creditor relationship - switches if there is aloan agreement etc

	- baillee realtionship could arise - safety deposit box

			- deposit made for a fixed rate interest.

				- contractual not fiduciary

				- limitation period ran from refused demand, not from time of deposit

			- contractual relationship goverend by implied and express terms
